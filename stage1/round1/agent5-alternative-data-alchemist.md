## HYPOTHESIS 1: FDA Briefing Document Asymmetric Skepticism

**HYPOTHESIS NAME:** FDA Briefing Document Asymmetric Skepticism

**SOURCE AGENT:** Alternative Data Alchemist

**MECHANISM:**
When FDA staff reviewers write advisory committee briefing documents ahead of PDUFA dates or adcom meetings, linguistic asymmetry between the benefit discussion and the risk discussion predicts a Complete Response Letter (CRL) or negative advisory committee vote. The causal chain: FDA reviewers are career regulators who cannot explicitly say "this drug should not be approved" in a public document without prejudicing the advisory committee process. Instead, they encode their skepticism through asymmetric language — hedging benefit claims with conditional and evidential constructions ("may provide benefit," "could be considered," "some evidence suggests") while stating safety concerns in definitive, unhedged language ("was associated with," "resulted in," "occurred in X% of patients"). This creates a measurable Benefit-Risk Linguistic Asymmetry: the benefit sections are syntactically complex, hedged, and cautious, while the risk sections are syntactically direct, declarative, and certain. When this asymmetry exceeds a threshold, it reveals the reviewer's internal negative stance 1-2 weeks before the formal decision, during which the biotech stock still trades near pre-decision prices.

**LLM ADVANTAGE:**
An LLM enables four things no human or traditional NLP can do simultaneously: (1) **Section-level asymmetry scoring** — the LLM segments the 50-200 page briefing document into benefit-discussion paragraphs and risk-discussion paragraphs (not trivially delineated by headers, as safety discussions bleed into efficacy sections), then computes a separate linguistic profile for each. (2) **Contextual hedging disambiguation** — traditional NLP counts hedging words but cannot distinguish "the drug may provide significant benefit, as demonstrated by the 40% reduction in events" (a confident statement using a conventional hedge) from "the drug may confer some benefit, though the magnitude is uncertain" (a genuinely skeptical hedge). The LLM reads the entire surrounding context to assign each hedge a skepticism score. (3) **Cross-document baseline comparison** — the LLM can process ALL FDA briefing documents across ALL drug reviews (n ~ 200-300 per year including non-adcom reviews) to build a distribution of normal reviewer language, so a given document's asymmetry score can be expressed in percentile terms relative to the population of FDA reviews. A human analyst reads 1-2 documents for their coverage; no one reads all of them systematically. (4) **Reviewer-division normalization** — different FDA review divisions (Oncology, Cardiovascular, Neurology) have different linguistic baselines. The LLM can compute division-specific z-scores so an Oncology reviewer's hedging isn't falsely flagged as skepticism just because Oncology reviewers are systematically more hedged.

**WHY UNDERWEIGHTED:**
(a) FDA briefing documents are public, free, and released on a deterministic schedule (typically 2 business days before the advisory committee meeting, or embedded in the review timeline for non-adcom PDUFAs). But they are 50-200 pages of dense medical, statistical, and regulatory prose that requires substantial domain expertise to parse. (b) Biotech analysts at investment banks read these documents for their coverage universe (5-15 names), but no one computes a cross-sectional linguistic asymmetry score across ALL drugs under review. The signal is invisible at the single-document level — you only see it when you compare a given document to the population distribution. (c) The academic literature on regulatory linguistics in drug approval exists (Batta et al., 2020; Lexchin & Mintzes, 2021) but has never been operationalized into a trading signal. The papers analyze FDA language retrospectively; no one has built a pre-decision predictive model. (d) The signal requires processing ~200+ lengthy PDF documents per year and computing nuanced linguistic metrics — before LLMs, this was a major research project. With LLMs, it is a weekend pipeline.

**HOLDING PERIOD:** 1-5 days (from briefing document publication to advisory committee vote, or to PDUFA date for drugs without adcom)

**DATA REQUIREMENT:**
- FDA Advisory Committee briefing documents (free, fda.gov) — published ~2 business days before adcom meetings; all briefing materials, including FDA staff review, sponsor presentation, and questions to the committee
- FDA Drugs@FDA database (free) — approval letters, Complete Response Letters (CRLs), review documents for all approved and non-approved drugs, searchable by application number
- FDA PDUFA date calendar (free, fda.gov and third-party aggregators like biopharmcatalyst.com) — upcoming decision dates
- SEC EDGAR (free) — for biotech company 10-K/10-Q risk factor sections, to cross-reference FDA status
- Yahoo Finance (free) — price data for all publicly traded biotech companies with upcoming PDUFA dates
- ClinicalTrials.gov (free) — confirmatory trial data to cross-check FDA briefing document claims against trial results

**PROCESSING APPROACH:**
1. **Document Acquisition and Segmentation:** For each upcoming PDUFA drug with a publicly traded sponsor (approximately 80-120 per year with adcom, 150-200 total PDUFAs), download the FDA briefing document when it becomes available. The LLM segments the document into paragraphs, then classifies each paragraph as: BENEFIT_DISCUSSION, RISK_DISCUSSION, BACKGROUND, STATISTICAL_METHODS, or OTHER. A paragraph is BENEFIT_DISCUSSION if it discusses efficacy endpoints, clinical benefit, or positive trial results. It is RISK_DISCUSSION if it discusses adverse events, safety signals, toxicity, or risk-benefit tradeoffs. Critical: paragraphs that discuss both are classified as RISK_DISCUSSION (conservative, since the FDA often embeds safety concerns within efficacy discussions).
2. **Linguistic Feature Extraction per Paragraph:**
   - **Hedging Density (HD):** Count of hedging constructions ("may," "might," "could," "potentially," "possible," "suggests," "appears to," "seems," "some evidence of," "trend toward") divided by total sentences in the paragraph.
   - **Certainty Markers (CM):** Count of definitive constructions ("demonstrated," "confirmed," "established," "was associated with," "resulted in," "occurred in," "is," "was shown to") divided by total sentences.
   - **Readability:** Flesch-Kincaid grade level of the paragraph (higher = more complex, typical of hedged/cautious language).
   - **Active-to-Passive Ratio:** Ratio of active-voice to passive-voice constructions. Passive voice is associated with distancing from claims.
   - **Numerical Precision:** Standard deviation of numbers cited (a reviewer who cites exact percentages and confidence intervals is more engaged/concerned than one who uses round numbers and vague quantifiers).
3. **Asymmetry Score Computation:** For each document, compute:
   - Mean hedging density in BENEFIT paragraphs: HD_benefit
   - Mean hedging density in RISK paragraphs: HD_risk
   - Mean certainty markers in BENEFIT paragraphs: CM_benefit
   - Mean certainty markers in RISK paragraphs: CM_risk
   - **Benefit-Risk Linguistic Asymmetry Score (BRLAS):** BRLAS = (HD_benefit - HD_risk) + (CM_risk - CM_benefit) + 0.5 × (Readability_benefit - Readability_risk)
   - Positive BRLAS means the reviewer hedges benefits more than risks and is more certain about risks than benefits — this is the skeptical pattern.
   - Compute BRLAS z-score relative to all historical FDA review documents from the same review division (to normalize for division-specific writing styles).
4. **Flagging:** Flag a drug as HIGH RISK when BRLAS z-score > +1.0 (top ~16% of documents, one-tailed). Flag as EXTREME RISK when BRLAS z-score > +1.5 (top ~7%). Direction: short the sponsor's stock (via put options to cap risk, given binary-outcome asymmetry) or exit existing long positions.
5. **Exit:** Close the position on the day of the FDA decision announcement. For drugs with adcom meetings, partial profit-taking after the adcom vote (which occurs ~1 day after the briefing document, and is a strong but imperfect predictor of the final FDA decision).

**FALSIFIABLE PREDICTION:**
If real, drugs flagged as EXTREME RISK (BRLAS z-score > +1.5) should receive a CRL or negative adcom vote at a rate of at least **50%**, compared to the unconditional CRL rate of approximately **15-20%** for drugs that reach the advisory committee stage (i.e., roughly a 2.5-3x lift in negative-outcome probability). Furthermore, the stock price reaction from briefing document publication to decision announcement for EXTREME RISK flags should average **-15% or worse** (capturing the market's gradual realization of negative risk even before the formal decision) compared to 0% to -5% for unflagged drugs. If fake, BRLAS z-scores above +1.5 should show no meaningful separation from the unconditional CRL rate (chi-squared test p > 0.15) and flagged-drug stock returns between publication and decision should be indistinguishable from unflagged drugs (t-test p > 0.10).

**MINIMUM EFFECT SIZE:**
The ability to avoid 1 in 3 CRLs (i.e., exit a long position or initiate a short/put before a -30% to -70% single-day crash) relative to an unconditional long-only biotech strategy. At a 5% portfolio allocation to each pre-PDUFA biotech position and 10-15 positions per year, avoiding one -40% crash per year saves 200-300bps of portfolio value annually. This is economically meaningful for a $50K-$250K portfolio and well exceeds transaction costs (put option premiums, which are elevated pre-catalyst but still cost ~5-8% of notional for near-the-money protection over a 1-week window).

**OUT-OF-SAMPLE PLAN:**
- **Training (Historical Baseline Construction):** Process ALL FDA advisory committee briefing documents from 2017-2022 (6 years, approximately 250-400 drugs). Build the division-specific BRLAS distribution (mean, SD, percentiles for Oncology, Cardiovascular, Neurology, Immunology, Endocrinology/Metabolism, and an "Other" bucket for rare diseases). Compute the baseline CRL rate unconditional vs. by BRLAS decile.
- **Calibration (Validation):** Apply the BRLAS scoring pipeline to 2023 calendar year drugs (approximately 40-60 adcom-track drugs). Tune the z-score threshold (+1.0, +1.5, +2.0) on this period. Also test: does the signal work equally well for small-cap (<$2B) vs. large-cap biotech? Does it work for non-adcom PDUFAs where only an internal FDA review document is available?
- **Out-of-Sample Test:** Apply the FIXED threshold to all 2024 PDUFA drugs. Report: (a) CRL rate for flagged vs. unflagged, (b) average stock return from briefing document publication to FDA decision for flagged vs. unflagged, (c) precision and recall of the signal (where precision = % of flagged drugs that actually received negative outcomes, recall = % of negative-outcome drugs that were flagged).
- **Walk-Forward 2025:** Re-estimate division-specific baselines annually (expanding window). Apply the frozen z-score threshold. Monitor whether the signal decays as the market potentially adapts (e.g., if sell-side analysts begin computing similar metrics and pricing them into pre-decision spreads).

**SELF-ASSESSED CONFIDENCE:** Medium-High

**BIGGEST WEAKNESS:**
(1) **Reviewer heterogeneity within divisions.** Even within the same division, different review teams have different writing styles. The division-level baseline may not fully normalize for this — a particularly cautious reviewer on Drug A produces a high BRLAS that reflects their personality, not the drug's problems. Mitigation: track individual reviewer baselines where author names are available (FDA briefing documents are sometimes signed, sometimes not). (2) **The "too clean" problem.** The drugs that reach the advisory committee stage are already triaged by the FDA. The worst drugs (clearly unsafe, clearly ineffective) never reach this stage because sponsors withdraw them or the FDA issues a refuse-to-file letter. The signal operates on a truncated sample of drugs that are already in the "maybe approvable" zone. The effect size may be smaller than claimed because the FDA has already filtered out the clearest negative signals. (3) **Market anticipation.** Option implied volatility for pre-PDUFA biotech names is already extremely elevated (150-300% IV is common). The put option cost may eat most or all of the edge — the signal must work net of pre-event option premiums, not just predict outcomes. (4) **Document format evolution.** FDA briefing document formats change over time (new templates, new review divisions formed, policy changes). The linguistic baselines computed on 2017-2022 documents may not be valid for 2024+ documents if the FDA's writing guidelines change materially.

## HYPOTHESIS 2: Job Posting Semantic Pivot as Strategic Inflection Signal

**HYPOTHESIS NAME:** Job Posting Semantic Pivot as Strategic Inflection Signal

**SOURCE AGENT:** Alternative Data Alchemist

**MECHANISM:**
A quarter-over-quarter shift in the semantic content of a public company's job postings — specifically, a change in the ratio of "exploratory/expansion" role language to "optimization/harvesting" role language at the product or business-unit level — predicts revenue growth acceleration or deceleration 1-2 quarters later. The causal chain: companies hire for what they intend to do 6-12 months out. Job posting language reveals unannounced strategic intent because hiring requisitions must be specific enough to attract qualified candidates, yet they are publicly visible before any formal strategic announcement. When job postings shift toward expansion language ("launch," "build from scratch," "greenfield," "new market," "0-to-1," "first [role]," "pioneer") in a given product area, it signals a growth investment cycle that will drive revenue in 2-4 quarters. Conversely, when job postings shift toward optimization language ("optimize," "streamline," "efficiency," "cost reduction," "automate," "consolidate," "mature," "refine") without corresponding new-product roles, it signals that management has shifted from growth to harvesting mode — a strategic contraction that precedes revenue deceleration by 1-2 quarters. The critical insight is that the *semantic direction* of hiring language changes before the *quantity* of hiring changes — companies often maintain headcount while pivoting what those heads will do, so job-count-based screens miss the signal entirely.

**LLM ADVANTAGE:**
An LLM enables three capabilities that are impossible with traditional methods: (1) **Strategic intent extraction from natural language** — an LLM reads the full job posting text and classifies the role's strategic function ("growth/expansion," "maintenance/optimization," "defensive/restructuring," "compliance/reporting") by understanding the semantic relationship between the described responsibilities and what they imply about company strategy. Traditional keyword counting cannot distinguish "we're hiring a Product Manager to launch our new AI features" (expansion) from "we're hiring a Product Manager to optimize our legacy platform" (harvesting) — both contain "Product Manager" and "feature/platform." The LLM understands that "launch new" vs. "optimize legacy" signals opposite strategic intents. (2) **Product-area semantic clustering** — the LLM clusters job postings by the product/business-unit they serve (e.g., "Cloud Infrastructure," "Consumer Mobile App," "Enterprise SaaS — SMB segment") based on the semantic content of the role description, not just department tags. This enables computing the expansion/harvesting ratio *per product area*, detecting when one division is pivoting while the company-wide aggregate looks stable. (3) **Emerging-role detection** — the LLM identifies new job roles that don't match predefined taxonomies ("AI Safety Researcher," "Prompt Engineer," "Generative Media Producer") and correctly classifies their strategic function based on role description semantics, rather than miscategorizing them or ignoring them.

**WHY UNDERWEIGHTED:**
(a) **Job posting counts are tracked; job posting semantics are not.** Several alternative data vendors (Revelio Labs, LinkUp, Thinknum) sell job posting count data to quant funds. But these datasets count postings by title/department/location, not by the *semantic content and strategic direction* of the posting text. No commercial dataset computes a "Strategic Pivot Index" from job posting language. (b) **Scale problem that LLMs solve.** A mid-cap company (~$2B-$10B market cap) might post 50-200 new roles per quarter. Across the Russell 3000, that's potentially 100,000-500,000 job postings per quarter. Before LLMs, processing this volume of natural language to extract strategic intent at the product-area level was cost-prohibitive — you'd need an army of human analysts reading job descriptions. (c) **Academic orphan.** The relationship between hiring and future performance is studied in management science (Powell & Snellman, 2004; Li, 2013) but focuses on hiring *quantity* (headcount growth) as a predictor, not the strategic content of hires. The linguistic dimension is unexplored. (d) **Free, public data with no vendor dependency.** Job postings are public by definition — companies post them to attract applicants. Scraping career pages and public job boards (LinkedIn, Indeed, Glassdoor) is rate-limited but feasible for a retail trader checking once per day. No need for expensive alternative data subscriptions.

**HOLDING PERIOD:** 1-6 months (time for strategic pivots to manifest in reported financials — revenue growth, R&D expense shifts, margin changes)

**DATA REQUIREMENT:**
- **Company career pages** (free) — direct source, most complete and timely for each company. A retail trader with a Python script can scrape ~200-500 target company career pages daily using requests/BeautifulSoup.
- **LinkedIn job postings** (free, public view) — broader coverage across companies. Rate-limited but sufficient for once-daily checking of ~200 companies.
- **Indeed.com job postings** (free, public) — supplementary source for cross-validation.
- **Glassdoor job listings** (free, public) — supplementary, often includes role descriptions not found elsewhere.
- **Internet Archive Wayback Machine** (free) — for historical career page snapshots, enabling backtesting. Spotty coverage but can capture major companies' career pages over multiple years.
- **Yahoo Finance** (free) — price data, revenue estimates, actual reported revenue.
- **SEC EDGAR** (free) — 10-K/10-Q for verifying reported segment revenue and headcount.
- **GICS sector/industry classification** — from Wikipedia or free financial data APIs.

**PROCESSING APPROACH:**
1. **Job Posting Collection:** For a target universe of the Russell 3000 (filtered to companies with >$500M market cap and active career pages), collect all active job postings once per week. Each posting is stored with: company, date collected, full text, URL, and inferred department/location from the posting metadata.
2. **LLM Classification — Strategic Intent:** For each job posting, the LLM classifies the role's strategic intent into one of five categories, producing a confidence score (0-1) for each:
   - **EXPANSION:** Role is for building something new — new product, new market, new capability. Language markers: "launch," "build from scratch," "greenfield," "first [role title]," "new team," "pioneer," "0-to-1," "create," "establish."
   - **GROWTH:** Role is for scaling an existing successful product/initiative. Language markers: "scale," "grow," "accelerate," "expand," "double," "fast-paced growth."
   - **MAINTENANCE:** Role is for operating/iterating existing products. Language markers: "maintain," "support," "iterate," "enhance," "improve," "manage," "oversee."
   - **OPTIMIZATION:** Role is explicitly for cost-cutting, efficiency, or consolidation. Language markers: "optimize," "streamline," "efficiency," "automate," "cost reduction," "consolidate," "standardize," "rationalize."
   - **DEFENSIVE:** Role is for compliance, risk management, or restructuring. Language markers: "compliance," "regulatory," "restructuring," "remediation," "litigation," "audit," "risk management."
   - (A role can have multiple labels; the LLM assigns the primary and secondary with confidence scores.)
3. **Product-Area Clustering:** Using embeddings of the job posting text (specifically the "responsibilities" and "qualifications" sections), cluster all of a company's job postings into product/business-unit clusters. Use cosine similarity with a threshold tuned to produce 3-10 clusters for a typical mid-cap company. The LLM then assigns each cluster a human-readable product-area label. This step ensures we measure strategic pivot *within* product areas, not company-wide (which could be diluted by a company simultaneously expanding in one division and harvesting in another).
4. **Strategic Pivot Index (SPI) Computation, per Product Area, per Quarter:**
   - For each product-area cluster at a given company, count the number of job postings classified in each strategic-intent category.
   - Compute the **Expansion Intensity (EI)** = (EXPANSION postings + 0.5 × GROWTH postings) / total postings in that product area.
   - Compute the **Optimization Intensity (OI)** = (OPTIMIZATION postings + DEFENSIVE postings) / total postings in that product area.
   - **Strategic Pivot Index (SPI)** = EI_current_quarter - EI_prior_quarter. Positive SPI = pivoting toward expansion. Negative SPI = pivoting toward optimization/harvesting.
   - Compute a **product-area-weighted company-level SPI** = sum over product areas of (SPI_i × revenue_weight_i), where revenue_weight is estimated from the most recent 10-K segment reporting. If segment data is not available, equal-weight the product areas.
5. **Signal Generation:**
   - **Long candidate (SPI > +0.15):** Company is pivoting toward expansion. The threshold of +0.15 means expansion-intensity increased by 15 percentage points QoQ (e.g., from 30% to 45% of postings in a given product area).
   - **Short/avoid candidate (SPI < -0.15):** Company is pivoting toward optimization/harvesting in a revenue-significant product area.
   - **Confidence adjustment:** Multiply SPI by the LLM's average classification confidence. A 15-point shift with 90% classification confidence is stronger than a 15-point shift with 60% confidence.
   - Filter: only generate signals when the product area represents >10% of estimated company revenue and the absolute number of postings in that product area is >5 (to avoid noise from small sample sizes).

**FALSIFIABLE PREDICTION:**
If real, companies with SPI > +0.15 (top ~15-20% of QoQ SPI changes, revenue-significant product area) should exhibit positive revenue surprises (actual revenue > consensus estimate by >1%) in the subsequent 1-2 quarterly reports at a rate at least **1.6x** the unconditional positive-surprise rate (baseline ~60-65%, so flagged should show >95%? No, that's too high. Let me be more precise.) The unconditional rate of >1% positive revenue surprises is approximately 30-35%. Flagged SPI-positive companies should show a >1% positive revenue surprise rate of **50% or higher** (roughly 1.5-1.7x lift). Conversely, companies with SPI < -0.15 (bottom 15-20%) should show a >1% negative revenue surprise rate of **35-40%** vs. unconditional ~25%. The effect should be asymmetric: expansion signals (SPI positive) should be more predictive than contraction signals (SPI negative) because expansion hiring takes time to translate to revenue, while contraction can be rapid (layoffs, project cancellations that immediately affect revenue).
Additional falsifiable prediction: the predictive power of SPI should decay with market cap — companies with market cap $500M-$5B should show the strongest relationship (less analyst coverage, more idiosyncratic strategies), while companies with market cap >$50B should show weak or no predictive power (too many business lines, job postings too granular to capture in aggregate).

If fake, SPI should have no predictive power for revenue surprises after controlling for: (a) prior-quarter revenue growth momentum, (b) analyst estimate revision breadth, (c) sector fixed effects (tech companies both hire more and grow faster — the signal would just be a noisy tech-sector proxy).

**MINIMUM EFFECT SIZE:**
Excess return of 300bps over a 3-month holding period relative to a size-and-sector-matched control portfolio, net of 50bps estimated transaction costs (slippage on mid-cap names, two round-trips per quarter). At $100K position size, this is $3,000 of excess return per position per quarter.

**OUT-OF-SAMPLE PLAN:**
- **Phase 1 (LLM Classification Validation):** Manually label 500 job postings across 20 companies and 5 sectors for strategic intent (EXPANSION, GROWTH, MAINTENANCE, OPTIMIZATION, DEFENSIVE). Compare LLM classifications against human labels. Achieve >80% agreement (Cohen's kappa > 0.70) before proceeding.
- **Phase 2 (Historical Backtest Construction):** Use the Internet Archive Wayback Machine to capture snapshots of career pages for S&P 500 and S&P 400 (MidCap) companies at quarterly intervals from 2019-2023. Career pages for large companies are typically archived multiple times per quarter. For companies with insufficient Wayback coverage, supplement with historical LinkedIn job posting data (available via free academic data-sharing agreements or Kaggle datasets). Construct a panel dataset of quarterly SPI scores and subsequent revenue surprises.
- **Phase 3 (Calibration):** On 2019-2022 data, calibrate: (a) the SPI threshold for signal generation (try +0.10, +0.15, +0.20), (b) the minimum number of postings per product area for signal generation, (c) the product-area revenue weight threshold for inclusion. Evaluate using simple long-short portfolio returns (top SPI quintile long, bottom SPI quintile short, sector-neutral).
- **Phase 4 (Out-of-Sample Test):** Apply the FIXED model to 2023 Q1-Q4 data. Report: (a) long-short decile spread returns, (b) revenue surprise rates by SPI quintile, (c) Fama-French 3-factor alpha of the long-short portfolio, (d) performance by market-cap quintile and by GICS sector. The critical test: does the long-short spread persist after controlling for price momentum, analyst revision breadth, and sector?
- **Phase 5 (Live Shadow Mode):** Run the pipeline in shadow mode on 2024 Q1-Q4 data. Simulate once-per-week portfolio rebalancing with realistic transaction costs. Compare predicted signals to actual subsequent revenue surprises. Deploy to live trading only if the shadow-mode information coefficient (IC) exceeds 0.05.

**SELF-ASSESSED CONFIDENCE:** Medium

**BIGGEST WEAKNESS:**
(1) **Internet Archive Wayback coverage is sparse and biased.** Large, well-known companies have good career page archival; small-caps and mid-caps have spotty coverage. The historical backtest may have severe survivorship and selection bias — the companies with good archival coverage are also the ones that survived and grew. The backtest results may significantly overstate the signal's effectiveness. (2) **Job posting staleness.** Career pages are not always updated promptly. A posting for an "expansion" role may have been filled months ago but left up, or a posting may be refreshed without changing the text. The signal relies on job posting text reflecting *current* strategic intent, which may lag actual strategic decisions by unknown periods. (3) **Strategic intent vs. actual outcome.** Companies hire for expansion but projects get cancelled. Companies hire for optimization but market conditions change and force growth investments. The signal captures *management's stated intent* through hiring, but management intent and actual outcomes diverge frequently. The signal may have high false-positive rates simply because corporate strategy is noisy. (4) **Competitive response.** If this signal works and becomes known, companies could game it by changing job posting language to signal expansion and boost their stock price. Since job postings are marketing as much as hiring documents, the incentive to manipulate is real.

## HYPOTHESIS 3: App Store Review Functional Failure Language as Quality Crisis Signal

**HYPOTHESIS NAME:** App Store Review Functional Failure Language as Quality Crisis Signal

**SOURCE AGENT:** Alternative Data Alchemist

**MECHANISM:**
When a publicly traded consumer-technology company's app store reviews shift from "preference-based dissatisfaction" (subjective complaints about design, pricing, or feature preferences) to "functional failure language" (objective reports of the product not working as advertised — crashes, data loss, billing errors, broken features, security concerns), the volume and velocity of this shift predicts elevated churn, reduced net revenue retention, warranty/remediation costs, and negative earnings surprises 1-2 quarters later. The causal chain: app store reviews are the closest thing to real-time, unsolicited customer feedback at scale. When a product has a quality crisis — a buggy release, a security vulnerability, a data corruption issue, a billing system error — affected users post reviews describing functional failure. These reviews are distinct from normal negative reviews (which complain about price increases, missing features, or UI preferences) because they describe the product *ceasing to perform its core function*. The distinction matters because: (a) functional failure drives churn much more than preference dissatisfaction (a user who says "I wish it had dark mode" stays; a user who says "it deleted my data" leaves immediately), (b) functional failure at scale creates balance-sheet liabilities (refunds, lawsuit risk, regulatory scrutiny), and (c) functional failure spreads virally (users warn other users), creating a demand shock beyond the directly affected users. The LLM distinguishes functional-failure reviews from preference-complaint reviews — a distinction that keyword approaches systematically fail on because both types use negative language, but the *causal relationship to churn* is radically different.

**LLM ADVANTAGE:**
An LLM enables four discriminative capabilities that keyword counting, sentiment analysis, and star-rating tracking cannot replicate: (1) **Functional failure detection vs. preference complaint.** Consider two 1-star reviews: "This app is terrible. I hate the new design. Bring back the old version." vs. "This app just charged me $99.99 three times and customer support won't respond." Both are 1-star negative reviews. A sentiment model scores both as "strongly negative." A keyword system flags both for "terrible" and "hate" vs. "charged" and "won't respond." But the LLM reads both and understands that the first signals personal taste (low churn risk), while the second signals a billing failure (high churn risk, potential regulatory issue). (2) **Crisis-type taxonomy.** The LLM classifies functional failures into fine-grained categories: BILLING_ERROR, DATA_LOSS, CRASH_BUG, SECURITY_BREACH, SYNC_FAILURE, LOGIN_BLOCK, CHARGED_NO_ACCESS, CORE_FEATURE_BROKEN. This taxonomy allows tracking which type of crisis is emerging (a billing error crisis has different financial implications than a crash-bug crisis) and thresholding differently for each. (3) **Crisis velocity measurement.** The LLM identifies when reviews describing the SAME functional failure begin clustering in time (e.g., 50 reviews in 3 days all describing "app crashes when I try to save") by embedding review text and detecting tight semantic clusters that appear abruptly. Traditional star-rating monitoring sees "average rating dropped from 4.2 to 3.8" — this lags the crisis by days or weeks because the installed base of old 5-star reviews takes time to dilute. The LLM detects the crisis from the *content* of the most recent reviews before the average rating moves. (4) **Cross-platform crisis detection.** Many consumer products have apps on both Apple App Store and Google Play Store, plus web reviews (Trustpilot, G2, Amazon). The LLM can detect when the same functional-failure cluster appears across multiple platforms simultaneously, filtering out platform-specific review bombing or fake reviews, and confirming a genuine product quality event.

**WHY UNDERWEIGHTED:**
(a) **Sentiment tools measure valence, not failure type.** App store review analytics is a mature industry (AppFollow, Appbot, Apptentive, data.ai), but these tools focus on star ratings, keyword frequency, and sentiment trends. None classify reviews by *functional failure vs. preference complaint* because that requires language understanding beyond sentiment analysis. Quant funds that buy app-store data get time-series of average ratings and review volumes — they miss the semantic content. (b) **Star ratings are a lagging indicator of product crisis.** The average star rating of an app with 100,000 reviews moves slowly because each new review is a tiny fraction of the total. A crisis that generates 500 1-star reviews in a week will move the average from 4.3 to 4.28 — invisible. But those 500 reviews contain specific functional-failure language that is detectable immediately. (c) **Cross-company comparability.** By building a functional-failure taxonomy that applies across all consumer-tech companies, the LLM enables comparison of crisis severity across companies (e.g., "Company A has a billing-error crisis affecting 5% of recent reviews" vs. "Company B has a crash-bug crisis affecting 3%"). This cross-sectional comparability is new. (d) **No existing quant factor.** To my knowledge, no published or commercial quant factor uses app store review functional-failure classification as a predictor of equity returns. It is a genuinely novel data-to-signal pathway.

**HOLDING PERIOD:** 1-4 weeks (crises unfold quickly — the market learns about product failures through social media and tech press within days to weeks of the initial review surge; the edge is in the 1-3 week window before formal company acknowledgment or sell-side downgrade)

**DATA REQUIREMENT:**
- **Apple App Store RSS feed** (free) — provides recent reviews for any public app. Rate-limited but sufficient for daily polling of ~500 apps. Each review includes: star rating, title, body text, date, version, and (optional) developer response.
- **Google Play Store reviews** (free, via google-play-scraper Python library or similar) — recent reviews with same fields. More generous rate limits than Apple.
- **Trustpilot** (free, public pages) — for consumer-finance, e-commerce, and service companies with Trustpilot presence. Reviews are public and scrapable.
- **G2 / Capterra** (free, public pages) — for B2B SaaS companies. Reviews are partially public.
- **Yahoo Finance** (free) — price data for publicly traded consumer-tech companies.
- **Company quarterly filings (SEC EDGAR, free)** — to verify reported metrics: net revenue retention, churn, MAU/DAU, and any disclosed "service disruption" or "security incident" costs.
- **GICS classification** — to identify consumer-tech subsectors: Interactive Media & Services, Application Software (consumer-facing), Entertainment, Consumer Electronics, Financial Technology (consumer apps).

**PROCESSING APPROACH:**
1. **Universe Selection:** Identify all publicly traded US companies whose primary product or a material revenue driver is a consumer-facing app. Universe includes: social media (META, SNAP, PINS), streaming (NFLX, SPOT), gaming (TTWO, EA, RBLX), consumer fintech (SQ, PYPL, COIN), delivery/rideshare (UBER, DASH), dating apps (MTCH, BMBL), fitness/wellness (PTON), and e-commerce with app presence (AMZN, ETSY, CHWY). Additional include: any company whose 10-K mentions "mobile application" or "app" as a primary customer interface. Estimated universe: 80-150 public companies.
2. **Daily Review Collection:** For each company's primary app(s) (identified via company IR page or 10-K), collect all new reviews posted in the past 24 hours from both Apple App Store and Google Play Store. Backfill 90 days of reviews for baseline. Target: collect 200-5,000 reviews per day across the universe.
3. **LLM Classification — Functional Failure Type:**
   For each review, the LLM classifies it into one primary category and optionally a secondary:
   - **FUNCTIONAL_FAILURE_BILLING:** Unauthorized charges, double charges, charged without receiving service, incorrect amounts, subscription cancellation failure.
   - **FUNCTIONAL_FAILURE_CRASH:** App crashes, freezes, won't open, force-closes during use, blank screens.
   - **FUNCTIONAL_FAILURE_DATA:** Data loss, files deleted, progress reset, settings wiped, account data corrupted.
   - **FUNCTIONAL_FAILURE_ACCESS:** Cannot log in, account locked, authentication failure, verification code not received.
   - **FUNCTIONAL_FAILURE_CORE:** Core feature broken — the primary function of the app does not work (e.g., "can't send messages" for a messaging app, "can't place orders" for a delivery app, "video won't play" for a streaming app).
   - **FUNCTIONAL_FAILURE_SECURITY:** Unauthorized account access, suspicious activity, privacy concern, data breach report.
   - **FUNCTIONAL_FAILURE_PERF:** Severe performance degradation — extreme slowness, battery drain, overheating, excessive data usage.
   - **PREFERENCE_COMPLAINT:** Negative review about design, missing features, pricing, policy changes, or personal preference. NOT a functional failure.
   - **OTHER:** Positive reviews, neutral reviews, off-topic, spam, non-English (flag for translation).
   The LLM provides a confidence score (0-1) and a 1-sentence justification for the classification.
4. **Crisis Velocity Computation:**
   - For each app, each day, compute the **Functional Failure Ratio (FFR)** = (number of FUNCTIONAL_FAILURE reviews of all subtypes) / (total reviews collected that day).
   - Compute the **Category-Specific FFR** for each failure subtype (e.g., FFR_billing, FFR_crash).
   - Compute the **30-day trailing average FFR** and the **30-day trailing standard deviation** of daily FFR.
   - **Crisis Signal:** A specific failure category fires a crisis signal when its daily FFR exceeds the 30-day trailing average by >3 standard deviations AND the absolute count of functional-failure reviews of that category on that day exceeds 10 (to prevent a single review triggering a signal on a low-volume app).
   - **Crisis Severity Score (CSS):** CSS = (daily FFR - trailing_avg_FFR) / trailing_std_FFR, winsorized at 6.0. This is a z-score representing how anomalous the current failure rate is relative to the app's normal baseline.
5. **Cross-Validation (False Positive Filter):**
   - Require that the crisis signal appears on AT LEAST TWO of the following platforms within a 3-day window: Apple App Store, Google Play Store, Trustpilot. This filters out platform-specific issues (e.g., Apple App Store review bombing) that don't reflect a genuine product problem.
   - Require that the crisis signal is not explained by a known app version release that would naturally generate transient issues (e.g., a major version update on the same day). If a version release occurred, flag the crisis as "possibly transient" and require 3 consecutive days of elevated FFR before firing.
6. **Position Sizing and Exit:**
   - **Direction:** Short (via put options) or exit existing long positions.
   - **Position size:** 3-5% of portfolio per crisis signal, given the high uncertainty of crisis duration and resolution.
   - **Exit conditions (whichever comes first):**
     - FFR returns to within 1 standard deviation of trailing average for 3 consecutive days (crisis resolving).
     - Company issues a formal acknowledgment (press release, 8-K, or app update notes) — at this point the information is public and priced in.
     - 3 calendar weeks have elapsed (crisis signal stale).
     - A competitor's app shows a symmetric spike in positive reviews mentioning switching from the affected company (competitive displacement signal — this is actually a reinforcing signal to stay in the position).

**FALSIFIABLE PREDICTION:**
If real, when a company's primary app fires a CSS > 3.0 (3+ sigma above trailing baseline, confirmed on 2+ platforms, >10 reviews), the company's stock should underperform its sector ETF by at least **400bps** over the subsequent 2-week period, with the underperformance concentrated in a 3-5 day window after the signal fires (as the crisis becomes visible to the market through social media and press). At least **60%** of crisis signals should produce negative excess returns over the holding period. Conversely, if fake, stocks with crisis signals should show returns indistinguishable from the sector ETF (mean excess return between -100bps and +100bps, p > 0.10 on a t-test of all crisis-signal events). Additionally, the magnitude of underperformance should be positively correlated with CSS (rho > 0.3) — more severe functional-failure outbreaks should produce larger negative returns.

**MINIMUM EFFECT SIZE:**
300bps excess negative return over 2 weeks relative to sector, net of 75bps estimated transaction costs (put option premium for a 2-week near-the-money put on a mid-cap consumer-tech name, typically elevated due to the nature of the signal being crisis-driven). At $100K notional short exposure via puts, this is $2,250-$3,000 of net profit per crisis event.

**OUT-OF-SAMPLE PLAN:**
- **Phase 1 (LLM Classification Benchmark):** Manually annotate 1,000 app store reviews (500 Apple, 500 Google Play) across 10 apps in 5 consumer-tech subsectors. Each review gets a gold-standard label for functional failure subtype (or preference complaint / other). Measure LLM classification accuracy, precision, and recall by subtype. Target: >85% F1 score for binary functional-failure vs. not, >75% F1 for specific subtype classification.
- **Phase 2 (Historical Baseline Construction):** Collect historical app store reviews for 20 major consumer-tech companies (2019-2023) using available public datasets (Kaggle app review datasets, academic data shares from papers that scraped app stores). Build per-app FFR baseline distributions. This is the hardest data collection step — app store review history is not perfectly archival. If full history is unavailable for backtesting, use the longest available lookback per app (minimum 12 months).
- **Phase 3 (Signal Calibration):** On 2019-2022 data (or longest available), identify all CSS > 2.0, >2.5, >3.0 events. For each event, measure forward 2-week stock returns relative to sector. Calibrate the CSS threshold and the minimum-review-count threshold to maximize the signal's precision (fraction of signals that produce negative excess returns) while maintaining at least 15-20 crisis signals per year (for a tradable opportunity set).
- **Phase 4 (Out-of-Sample Test):** Apply the FIXED thresholds to 2023-2024 data. Report: (a) number of crisis signals fired per year, (b) hit rate (% of signals with negative excess returns), (c) average excess return per signal, (d) Sharpe ratio of the crisis-signal short strategy, (e) CSS decile analysis — monotonic relationship between crisis severity and forward returns?
- **Phase 5 (Robustness Checks):**
  - **Platform specificity test:** Does the signal work equally well for Apple App Store-only crises vs. cross-platform crises? Hypothesis: cross-platform crises should be stronger.
  - **Company size test:** Does the signal work better for small/mid-cap consumer-tech (less analyst coverage, slower market reaction) vs. large-cap (more eyes on the product, faster price discovery)?
  - **Crisis-type decomposition:** Which failure subtypes (billing, data loss, security) produce the strongest and most persistent negative returns? Hypothesis: DATA_LOSS and SECURITY crises should produce the largest and most persistent effects because they create legal/regulatory liability beyond immediate churn.
  - **News contamination test:** For each crisis signal, check if the stock price already moved BEFORE the crisis signal fired (i.e., did the market learn about the problem through other channels first?). Use Factiva or Google News to find the first news article mentioning the product issue. If the news preceded the app store signal in >30% of cases, the signal is not leading-edge — it's coincident or lagging.

**SELF-ASSESSED CONFIDENCE:** Medium

**BIGGEST WEAKNESS:**
(1) **App store reviews are not a representative sample of users.** Users who post reviews are self-selected — they tend to be either extremely satisfied or extremely dissatisfied. The baseline functional-failure ratio in reviews may overstate the actual incidence of functional failure in the user base by 10-100x. This doesn't invalidate the signal (the *change* in failure rate is what matters, not the absolute level), but it means the CSS thresholds are calibrated on a noisy, skewed distribution. (2) **Astroturfing and review manipulation.** Companies actively manage their app store reviews — soliciting positive reviews, flagging negative reviews for removal, and potentially posting fake positive reviews to bury negative ones. The signal must account for the possibility that a crisis is artificially suppressed in the review data by the company's review-management efforts. The LLM could be trained to detect review authenticity (bot detection from linguistic patterns), but this adds complexity. (3) **Market anticipation through other channels.** Functional failure crises often become visible through social media (Twitter/X, Reddit, TikTok) simultaneously with or before they appear in app store reviews. The app-store signal may not be the *earliest* signal — Twitter sentiment or Reddit complaint volume might lead by hours to days. A comprehensive system would monitor both. (4) **Public company app review volume is highly variable.** Apps with 1M+ daily active users may get 100-500 reviews per day (signal rich). Apps with 50K DAU may get 1-5 reviews per day (signal poor, noise dominates). Many public consumer-tech companies fall in the low-review-volume zone where the signal cannot operate reliably. (5) **Short-selling friction.** Many consumer-tech names with app-store presence are high-growth, high-multiple stocks that are expensive to short (borrow costs >5% annualized, potential for short squeezes). Put options on volatile consumer-tech names carry high implied volatility premiums. The post-cost return may be significantly lower than the gross signal.

## Cross-Hypothesis Notes

**Domain Coverage:** These three hypotheses span three distinct alternative-data domains — regulatory/clinical documents (H1), human capital/strategic language (H2), and consumer-product feedback (H3). They are designed to be mutually independent: a biotech CRL, a strategic pivot at a software company, and a product crisis at a consumer-tech company are uncorrelated event types, so a trader could run all three signals simultaneously for diversification.

**LLM Cost Unification:** All three hypotheses share the same LLM infrastructure pattern: classification of unstructured text into domain-specific taxonomies. The prompt engineering for H1 (FDA document sentiment), H2 (job posting strategic intent), and H3 (review functional failure type) follows the same architecture — zero-shot or few-shot classification with confidence scores. A retail trader can use a single LLM API endpoint with different system prompts per signal, reducing complexity and cost. Estimated total LLM API cost across all three signals: $80-200/month (assuming once-daily batch processing of new documents/reviews/postings).

**Retail Trader Feasibility ($50K-$250K capital):**
- **Time commitment:** All three signals require once-daily checking. H1 fires around PDUFA dates (predictable, ~10-20 events per month). H2 fires weekly (scan SPI changes across 200-500 companies, ~5-15 signals per week). H3 fires when crises emerge (unpredictable, ~10-30 signals per year). A retail trader can run the pipeline in the morning (30-60 minutes of automated processing), review signals, and place orders during market hours.
- **Execution:** H1 uses put options for defined-risk short exposure on binary events. H2 uses long equity positions (no shorting cost) for expansion signals and avoids/sells existing positions for contraction signals. H3 uses put options or inverse sector ETFs for short exposure. All execution is achievable with a standard brokerage (Interactive Brokers, Schwab, Fidelity).
- **Position sizing:** 3-5% of portfolio per signal, 10-20 concurrent positions, sector diversification enforced. Maximum drawdown from any single position is capped by the position size (3-5% × max loss on a single name).

**Data Collection Feasibility:**
- H1 data (FDA documents) is fully archival and free — every historical document is available on fda.gov. This is the easiest hypothesis to backtest.
- H2 data (job postings) is partially archival via Internet Archive. Historical backtesting is feasible but noisy. Live-forward testing may be more practical.
- H3 data (app store reviews) is the hardest to backtest historically but the easiest to collect in real time. Historical review data exists in academic datasets and Kaggle but may not cover all target companies.
