# Agent 3: Narrative Economist — Round 1 Hypotheses

## Hypothesis 1: Q&A Coherence Decay Predicts Earnings Surprises

**HYPOTHESIS NAME:** Q&A Coherence Decay (QACD)

**SOURCE AGENT:** Narrative Economist

**MECHANISM:** During earnings calls, the prepared remarks section is scripted, lawyered, and polished. The Q&A section forces management to answer without a script. When management's Q&A answers show progressively declining semantic coherence with the prepared remarks -- measured as the trajectory of embedding cosine similarity between each sequential management response and the prepared remarks text -- this reveals that the prepared narrative was brittle spin rather than durable substance. Companies in the top quintile of Coherence Decay (largest drop in similarity from early Q&A to late Q&A) experience negative earnings surprises (actual EPS below consensus) in the subsequent quarter because the prepared narrative masked fundamental deterioration that crumbles under analyst probing. The causal chain: brittle narrative -> Q&A incoherence -> masked problems -> future earnings miss.

**LLM ADVANTAGE:** A human analyst listens to the call and forms a qualitative impression of whether management "sounded credible." An LLM can: (a) embed every sentence of prepared remarks and every management Q&A response as dense vectors; (b) compute pairwise cosine similarity between each sequential Q&A response and the prepared remarks centroid embedding; (c) fit a linear slope to the similarity trajectory across the 5-15 management responses in a typical Q&A; (d) do this across 10,000+ earnings calls per quarter. The trajectory measurement -- not just "are answers similar to prepared remarks" but "does similarity decay as the call progresses" -- requires computing structured similarity scores across an ordered sequence of unstructured text pairs, which traditional NLP (keyword overlap, sentiment scores, regex patterns) cannot capture. Humans can do it qualitatively for one call; LLMs can do it quantitatively at scale.

**WHY UNDERWEIGHTED:** (1) The signal requires measuring a within-call trajectory (decay slope), not a single summary statistic. Quant funds that process earnings calls typically extract aggregate sentiment or readability scores -- the temporal structure of Q&A coherence is ignored. (2) The signal is subtle: a -0.02 cosine similarity slope over 12 responses, not an obvious "the CEO is lying" tell. Humans lack the precision to measure slopes from unstructured speech. (3) The mechanism crosses linguistics, social psychology (impression management theory), and accounting -- genuinely interdisciplinary. (4) The holding period (next quarter's earnings, 1-4 months) is too long for high-frequency firms and too short for most fundamental funds, leaving a neglected horizon.

**HOLDING PERIOD:** 1-6 months (from earnings call date to next quarter's earnings announcement, typically 60-100 days)

**DATA REQUIREMENT:**
- **Earnings call transcripts:** Seeking Alpha transcripts (free tier, ~4,000+ US equities covered). Each transcript cleanly separates Prepared Remarks from Q&A, and identifies speaker roles (CEO, CFO, Analyst name/firm). Coverage: 2010-present. Frequency: quarterly per stock. Estimated corpus: ~200,000 transcripts.
- **Consensus earnings estimates:** Yahoo Finance (free, analyst estimates page) or Financial Modeling Prep free tier for historical consensus EPS estimates. Weekly or daily snapshots.
- **Actual reported earnings:** SEC EDGAR 8-K filings (free) or Yahoo Finance earnings history (free). Exact report date and reported EPS.
- **Price data:** Yahoo Finance daily adjusted close (free). Used for calculating earnings surprises and abnormal returns.
- **Control variables:** Market cap (Yahoo Finance), sector (GICS from SEC filings or Yahoo Finance profile), historical earnings surprise magnitude and frequency.

**PROCESSING APPROACH:**
1. **Segment:** Parse transcript into Prepared Remarks (all CEO+CFO speech before first analyst question) and Q&A management responses (each CEO/CFO answer block, preserving order).
2. **Embed:** Use a sentence-transformer model (e.g., all-MiniLM-L6-v2, temperature=0 for reproducibility) to embed the full Prepared Remarks text as a single document embedding, and each sequential Q&A management response as individual embeddings.
3. **Compute trajectory:** For each Q&A response i = 1,2,...,n, compute cosine_sim(response_i, prepared_remarks_centroid). Fit OLS regression: similarity_i = alpha + beta * i + epsilon_i. The Coherence Decay Score (CDS) = -beta (positive CDS means declining coherence).
4. **Normalize:** Z-score CDS within each stock's own history (to control for management style -- some CEOs always drift more than others) and within each sector-earnings-season cohort (to control for macro narrative themes).
5. **Rank:** Split into quintiles. Top quintile = "High Decay" signal.
6. **Control variables:** Compute for each call: length of Q&A, number of analyst questions, earnings surprise from the *current* quarter (to verify decay is not just a mechanical reaction to a bad quarter already known), market cap, sector.

**FALSIFIABLE PREDICTION:** If QACD is a real edge, then: (a) stocks in the top quintile of within-stock-normalized CDS should show a mean subsequent-quarter earnings surprise (actual minus consensus, scaled by price) of at most -1.5 standard deviations below the stock's own historical surprise distribution, and this should hold in at least 13 out of any rolling 20-quarter window (binomial test p < 0.05 vs. null of 50% of quarters). (b) A long-short portfolio (short top CDS quintile, long bottom CDS quintile, held from call date + 1 day to the day before next earnings announcement) should generate annualized alpha > 4% after transaction costs, with the majority of alpha coming from the short side. If QACD is fake, we would observe: (a) the top-quintile mean surprise indistinguishable from zero or from the middle quintiles; (b) the decay slope driven primarily by call length or number of questions rather than actual semantic divergence; (c) no predictive power once controlling for the current quarter's earnings surprise magnitude.

**MINIMUM EFFECT SIZE:** Annualized long-short alpha > 4% post-cost (commissions + spread), or equivalently, the top-quintile short leg generates at least -3% abnormal return over the 60-100 day holding period. Information Coefficient (rank correlation between CDS quintile and subsequent earnings surprise) > 0.05. Below these thresholds, the signal is economically indistinguishable from noise after the opportunity cost of capital commitment.

**OUT-OF-SAMPLE PLAN:**
- **Temporal split:** Train the CDS normalization parameters and quintile breakpoints on 2010-2019 data. Validate on 2020-2024 (including COVID disruption as a stress test).
- **Rolling walk-forward:** Simulate deployment: at each earnings season, compute CDS for all calls in that season using only data available up to that date, form portfolios, track performance. This tests whether the signal would have been executable in real time.
- **Cross-sectional holdout:** Fit the embedding model once on a fixed corpus (e.g., all 2010-2018 transcripts) and do not retrain or fine-tune. Test whether the embedding similarity measurement generalizes to 2019-2024 transcripts without adaptation.
- **Sector randomization test:** Shuffle CDS scores across stocks within the same sector and verify the signal disappears -- confirming the signal lives at the stock level, not the sector level.

**SELF-ASSESSED CONFIDENCE:** Medium. The linguistic mechanism is theoretically well-grounded (impression management in accounting literature; the "script exhaustion" hypothesis from communications research). The empirical question is whether the within-call decay trajectory contains independent signal beyond what is already captured by the current quarter's earnings surprise and analyst tone.

**BIGGEST WEAKNESS:** Management teams may adapt once the signal is known. If CFOs become aware that Q&A coherence decay is being measured, they can train to maintain consistent semantic alignment throughout Q&A, which would erode the signal. Second, the signal may be concentrated in a subset of industries (e.g., tech where narratives matter more) or management styles (e.g., founder-CEOs who improvise more), limiting universality. Third, the decay trajectory might simply proxy for "the call went badly" which is already priced by the next trading day -- the real question is whether the decay predicts the *next* quarter's surprise beyond what the current quarter's price reaction already captures.


## Hypothesis 2: Analyst Question Homogeneity as Fragility Signal

**HYPOTHESIS NAME:** Analyst Question Cartel (AQC)

**SOURCE AGENT:** Narrative Economist

**MECHANISM:** Sell-side analysts collectively determine the thematic agenda of earnings calls through their questions. When multiple analysts on the same call ask questions that are semantically highly similar to each other (measured by mean pairwise embedding cosine similarity of analyst questions), this "question cartel" indicates that the sell-side is in groupthink -- all fixated on the same 1-2 themes to the exclusion of other potential risk factors or opportunity areas. This narrative homogeneity makes the stock fragile to information that falls outside the consensus frame. Specifically: when AQC scores are in the top quintile for a given stock AND the stock has moved >5% in the direction consistent with the dominant question theme in the 5 trading days before the call, the stock experiences mean reversion over the following 1-4 weeks. The mechanism: groupthink creates a one-dimensional market narrative; any news that doesn't fit the frame is initially ignored (enabling the pre-call trend) but then causes narrative collapse (triggering reversal) when it can no longer be dismissed. High homogeneity = fragile consensus = reversal-prone.

**LLM ADVANTAGE:** The core computation requires (a) identifying that two questions are "about the same thing" even when phrased differently ("How should we think about your CapEx trajectory for the next 2 years?" and "Can you give us some color on the spending outlook going forward?" are semantically nearly identical but share zero keywords beyond stop words); (b) computing all O(n^2) pairwise similarities among the 10-30 analyst questions on a call; (c) comparing the resulting homogeneity score to the stock's historical distribution and to cross-sectional peers. A human cannot compute pairwise semantic similarity among 20 questions in their head and compare to a historical baseline. Rules-based NLP (TF-IDF cosine, LDA topic models) would miss semantic equivalence across different phrasings. Traditional quant models don't ingest unstructured Q&A text at all. This is a pure LLM-native measurement: dense embeddings + pairwise similarity matrix + historical normalization.

**WHY UNDERWEIGHTED:** (1) Analyst herding is a known phenomenon, but measuring it via semantic question similarity on earnings calls (rather than via recommendation clustering or estimate dispersion) is novel and unmeasured. (2) The signal requires joint conditioning: high homogeneity AND a recent price trend in the theme-consistent direction. Simple homogeneity alone is not predictive -- it is the interaction with price momentum that creates the fragility setup. Interaction effects are systematically underexploited because they require measuring two entirely different types of data (text embeddings and price time series) and conditioning one on the other. (3) The signal is contrarian (fading consensus) and most institutional managers are benchmark-relative and career-risk-averse -- betting against consensus when consensus appears unified is psychologically difficult even if empirically justified. (4) The holding period (1-4 weeks) is too short for most fundamental funds and too long for market-makers, creating a horizon gap.

**HOLDING PERIOD:** 1-4 weeks (5-20 trading days post-call)

**DATA REQUIREMENT:**
- **Earnings call transcripts:** Seeking Alpha transcripts (free tier). Requires clean speaker identification to isolate analyst questions (all speech between "Your first question comes from..." and the subsequent management answer). Coverage: all US equities with analyst coverage, 2010-present. ~200,000 calls.
- **Price data:** Yahoo Finance daily adjusted close (free). Pre-call 5-day return, post-call 5/10/20-day returns.
- **Company metadata:** Market cap (to control for size -- small caps may have fewer analysts, mechanically affecting homogeneity), sector (GICS), analyst coverage count.
- **Short interest data:** FINRA short interest (free, bi-monthly) or Yahoo Finance short interest (free) to control for the possibility that high homogeneity simply reflects heavily shorted stocks where analysts are focused on the same bear thesis.

**PROCESSING APPROACH:**
1. **Extract questions:** Parse transcript to identify all analyst questions (text between analyst identification and management response). Exclude introductory pleasantries ("Thanks for taking my question"). Concatenate multi-part questions from the same analyst into a single question text.
2. **Embed questions:** Use a sentence-transformer model (all-MiniLM-L6-v2, temperature=0) to embed each analyst question as a dense vector.
3. **Compute homogeneity:** For a call with N analyst questions, compute the NxN pairwise cosine similarity matrix. The raw Question Homogeneity Score (QHS) = mean of the upper triangle (excluding diagonal). This is mean pairwise similarity.
4. **Normalize:** Z-score QHS within each stock's own call history (minimum 8 prior calls required for baseline). Also compute sector-date cohort Z-score to control for macro themes that affect all stocks simultaneously (e.g., "everyone asks about tariffs this quarter").
5. **Identify dominant theme:** Cluster question embeddings (HDBSCAN or agglomerative clustering) to identify the dominant thematic cluster. Classify the dominant theme's directional implication for the stock price (positive, negative, or neutral) using an LLM classifier with structured output. Only consider calls where the dominant theme has a clear directional implication (>70% of questions in dominant cluster share directional valence).
6. **Joint signal:** Flag calls where (a) within-stock-normalized QHS > 80th percentile (top quintile of homogeneity for that stock) AND (b) 5-day pre-call return is > +5% (if dominant theme is positive) or < -5% (if dominant theme is negative) AND (c) at least 5 analysts asked questions.
7. **Trade:** If dominant theme is positive -> short (or avoid if no short access). If dominant theme is negative -> long. Exit after 20 trading days or at next material event.

**FALSIFIABLE PREDICTION:** If AQC is a real edge, then: (a) the flagged signal condition should predict 20-day post-call return in the OPPOSITE direction of the pre-call trend with hit rate > 55% (vs. null of 50%), with mean reversal magnitude > 3% for the top signal quintile; (b) the signal strength should increase monotonically with QHS quintile (higher homogeneity -> larger reversal); (c) the effect should persist after controlling for analyst coverage count (fewer analysts mechanically enables higher similarity but should not predict reversal by itself). If AQC is fake, we would observe: (a) reversal frequency indistinguishable from 50%; (b) QHS merely proxying for "stocks with few analysts" with no independent predictive power; (c) the short-sale constraint making the short leg economically unexecutable for retail (no borrow available for the most homogeneous calls because they tend to be heavily shorted already).

**MINIMUM EFFECT SIZE:** Hit rate on directional reversal > 55% (vs. 50% null) with mean absolute 20-day return > 3% in the reversal direction. Annualized Sharpe > 0.4 after costs on the long-short portfolio. Below hit rate of 53% or absolute mean reversal < 2%, the signal has insufficient edge to overcome transaction costs and the occasional large trend-continuation loss.

**OUT-OF-SAMPLE PLAN:**
- **Temporal split:** Calibrate QHS normalization parameters and quintile boundaries on 2010-2019. Test on 2020-2024.
- **Sector-blind test:** Run the signal separately within each GICS sector to verify it is not driven by a single sector with unusual analyst behavior.
- **Random analyst dropout:** For each call, randomly drop 30% of questions and recompute QHS. The signal should be robust to this perturbation -- if it disappears, the signal is an artifact of small question counts rather than genuine homogeneity.
- **Placebo dates:** Compute QHS for the same stock on random non-earnings-call dates (using the most recent call's questions) and verify no predictive power -- confirming the signal is specific to the earnings call context where analyst groupthink is most consequential.
- **False discovery rate:** Apply Benjamini-Hochberg correction across all tested sector-time cohorts to ensure the signal survives multiple comparison adjustment.

**SELF-ASSESSED CONFIDENCE:** Medium-Low. The psychological mechanism (groupthink -> fragility) is compelling and has analogs in other domains (political polling herding, VC investment fads), but the translation to earnings call question similarity is speculative. The biggest risk is that question homogeneity is endogenous -- analysts ask similar questions because the company's situation genuinely warrants it, not because of groupthink. Distinguishing "warranted consensus" from "fragile groupthink" may be impossible from text alone, requiring the interaction with price trend as a partial filter.

**BIGGEST WEAKNESS:** Endogeneity. When a company genuinely faces one overwhelmingly important issue (e.g., a DOJ investigation, a patent cliff, a transformative acquisition), ALL analysts SHOULD ask about it. High question homogeneity in this case reflects rational information demand, not fragile groupthink, and the consensus is likely correct. The price-trend interaction filter helps (if the issue is truly dominant, the price move may be justified and not reverse), but the filter is crude. A deeper weakness: the LLM classifying the dominant theme's "directional implication" may hallucinate or misclassify neutral/complex themes as directional, creating noise in the signal. Third, the signal requires short-selling for the long-short portfolio, and the most homogeneous calls may coincide with hard-to-borrow names (heavily shorted stocks where everyone is asking the same bearish questions).


## Hypothesis 3: Management Credibility Trajectory from Multi-Event Statement Tracking

**HYPOTHESIS NAME:** Management Credibility Trajectory (MCT)

**SOURCE AGENT:** Narrative Economist

**MECHANISM:** Every time management makes forward-looking quantitative statements on earnings calls (guidance ranges, growth projections, margin targets, capex plans, product launch timelines), they create a testable prediction about their own future. Over successive calls, a management team accumulates a track record of accuracy that constitutes their "credibility trajectory." When a management team with a HIGH credibility trajectory (top quintile of statement-to-actual accuracy over the prior 8 quarters, recency-weighted) makes new forward-looking statements with high specificity (concrete numbers or narrow ranges, not vague aspirations), those stocks outperform over the next 1-6 months because: (a) the market systematically underweights management track record when reacting to guidance, overweighting the guidance magnitude and underweighting the guidance source reliability; (b) high-credibility managers' guidance contains more private information; (c) low-credibility managers' guidance is already discounted by sophisticated investors but not by the marginal retail/institutional investor who reacts to the headline guidance number. The credibility signal is orthogonal to the guidance *direction* -- it is about the *reliability* of the information source, which the market impounds incompletely.

**LLM ADVANTAGE:** This requires three capabilities that only an LLM can combine: (1) **Structured extraction from unstructured speech:** Parse a transcript sentence like "We expect revenue in the second half to come in somewhere between $2.4 and $2.6 billion, reflecting the ramp in our enterprise segment" into a structured record {metric: "revenue", value_range: [2.4e9, 2.6e9], timeframe: "H2 2026", confidence_markers: ["expect", "somewhere between"], specificity: "narrow_range"}. Traditional regex/NER fails because the phrasing varies enormously across managers, companies, and industries. (2) **Cross-document matching:** Match the extracted statement to the subsequently reported actual from a different document (10-Q, 10-K, 8-K) that uses different formatting, different segment definitions, and different terminology. "Revenue in the second half" in the call transcript must be matched to "Revenues for the six months ended December 31" in the 10-Q. (3) **Scale:** Do this for every forward-looking statement made by every management team across the Russell 3000 over a decade -- millions of statement-to-actual pairs. A human can track credibility for maybe 20 companies qualitatively; an LLM can do it quantitatively for 3,000.

**WHY UNDERWEIGHTED:** (1) The computation is absurdly labor-intensive for humans: extracting statements, tracking them for 6-18 months, matching to actuals, maintaining a running score, and doing this across a broad universe. The cost of doing this manually dwarfs any conceivable alpha. (2) Academic research on management credibility exists (e.g., Hutton & Stocken 2009, Rogers & Stocken 2005) but uses coarse proxies (historical forecast error frequency) rather than statement-level tracking. The fine-grained, statement-by-statement credibility score is a richer signal. (3) The market reacts to guidance *magnitude* (beat/miss vs. consensus) but the *source credibility* component requires tracking across multiple events spanning years -- most investors and quant models use the current guidance number in isolation, not conditioned on the speaker's track record. (4) LLMs changed the cost curve from "impossible" to "automated pipeline" -- this is a post-LLM opportunity that pre-LLM market structure never had a chance to price.

**HOLDING PERIOD:** 1-6 months (from the call date to either the next earnings announcement or the guidance timeframe endpoint, whichever comes first)

**DATA REQUIREMENT:**
- **Earnings call transcripts:** Seeking Alpha (free tier), 2010-present. Need full text with speaker identification (CEO, CFO).
- **SEC filings for verification:** 10-K and 10-Q filings from SEC EDGAR (free). Used to extract actual reported financials to verify whether management's prior statements materialized.
- **8-K filings:** SEC EDGAR (free). For interim guidance updates and material event disclosures that may confirm or contradict prior statements.
- **Price data:** Yahoo Finance daily adjusted close (free).
- **Consensus estimates:** Yahoo Finance analyst estimates (free) or Financial Modeling Prep (free tier) for benchmark comparison.
- **Statement tracking database:** A constructed database (the core LLM output) of structured forward-looking statements with fields: stock, call_date, speaker, metric, value_range, timeframe, specificity_score, confidence_markers, and -- once verified -- actual_outcome, accuracy_score, verification_date.

**PROCESSING APPROACH:**
1. **Statement extraction:** For each earnings call transcript, use an LLM (deterministic, temperature=0) to extract all forward-looking quantitative statements from management (CEO and CFO). Structured JSON output per statement: {speaker, metric_category (revenue/earnings/margin/capex/growth_rate/product_timeline/other), metric_specific, value_type (point/narrow_range/wide_range/directional_only), value_low, value_high, timeframe_start, timeframe_end, confidence_markers (expect/anticipate/target/on_track/confident/estimated/believe), specificity_score (1-5 scale based on value_type + confidence_markers)}.
2. **Verification matching:** For each statement whose timeframe_end has passed, search subsequent SEC filings (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K) for the corresponding actual reported metric. Use LLM to match despite formatting differences. Compute accuracy_score: for range forecasts, 1.0 if actual falls within range, 0.5 if within 10% of range boundary, 0.0 otherwise. For point forecasts, 1.0 if within 2% of forecast, decaying to 0.0 at 20% deviation.
3. **Credibility score construction:** For each management team (defined at the CEO+CFO pair level, since credibility is team-specific), compute the Credibility Score (CS) as an exponentially weighted moving average of accuracy_scores over the prior 8 quarters. EWMA decay factor = 0.7 (quarterly), so the most recent quarter gets ~40% weight and quarters 7-8 ago get ~3% combined weight. Require minimum 5 verified statements for a valid CS.
4. **New statement signal:** When a management team with CS > 0.80 (top quintile) makes a new forward-looking statement with specificity_score >= 4 (narrow range or point estimate with concrete confidence markers), flag the stock. The signal direction matches the statement direction: if the statement implies results above consensus -> long; below consensus -> short/avoid.
5. **Signal strength weighting:** Weight the position by (CS - 0.5) * specificity_score * statement_materiality (absolute dollar magnitude of guided metric relative to market cap). Higher credibility + higher specificity + more material statement = larger position.
6. **Decay monitoring:** Recompute CS after each new earnings call. If CS drops below 0.65 (a "credibility breach"), close any positions predicated on that management's credibility.

**FALSIFIABLE PREDICTION:** If MCT is a real edge, then: (a) a long-only portfolio of stocks where high-credibility managers (CS > 0.80) issue specific positive guidance should generate annualized alpha of at least 5% over the equal-weighted sector benchmark over a 1-6 month holding period, with the alpha concentrated in the first 3 months post-statement; (b) stocks with low-credibility managers (CS < 0.40) issuing specific positive guidance should show zero or negative alpha over the same horizon -- the credibility score matters, not just the guidance itself; (c) the spread between high-CS and low-CS guidance-following portfolios should be monotonically increasing across CS quintiles. If MCT is fake, we would observe: (a) no monotonic relationship between CS quintile and post-guidance alpha; (b) the signal completely subsumed by guidance magnitude -- i.e., managers who give accurate guidance are simply those who guide conservatively (lowball), and the alpha is the standard "beat conservative guidance" effect with no incremental value from credibility tracking; (c) credibility scores being highly autocorrelated but non-predictive (some managers always score high because they give vague statements that are always "in range", some always score low because they give specific statements that are occasionally wrong).

**MINIMUM EFFECT SIZE:** Annualized long-only alpha > 5% over sector benchmark for the top CS quintile. Information Coefficient (rank correlation between CS quintile and 3-month forward return, conditioned on guidance being issued) > 0.04. The purely long-only version (no shorting required, since retail short-sale constraints are binding) must still generate > 3% annualized alpha after costs.

**OUT-OF-SAMPLE PLAN:**
- **Temporal split:** Build the credibility score database on 2010-2019 calls and verifications. Test signal performance on 2020-2024. The COVID period provides a crucial stress test -- management credibility should be especially valuable during regime uncertainty.
- **Walk-forward simulation:** At each earnings call date, use only the CS computed from prior statements verified as of that date. No look-ahead. This is the true out-of-sample test because each new call's signal depends on a credibility database that was frozen at that moment in time.
- **Guidance-only control:** Compare the MCT signal to a naive "follow all guidance" strategy to isolate the incremental value of the credibility filter.
- **Specificity-only control:** Compare to a "follow only specific guidance" strategy (no credibility filter) to verify that credibility adds value beyond specificity.
- **CEO departure test:** When a CEO changes, the credibility score should reset (new CEO, unknown credibility). Verify that post-CEO-change signals using the old CS have no predictive power, confirming the signal is person-specific, not firm-specific.

**SELF-ASSESSED CONFIDENCE:** Medium-High. This is the most grounded of the three hypotheses because it directly extends well-established accounting research on management forecast credibility, and the LLM's role is to operationalize something that was previously only measurable with coarse proxies or tiny hand-collected samples. The causal mechanism (market underweights source reliability relative to signal content) has extensive psychology literature support (source credibility effect from persuasion research). The main risk is implementation complexity -- the extraction and matching pipeline has many steps where errors compound.

**BIGGEST WEAKNESS:** Implementation complexity and error propagation. The pipeline has multiple stages (extract -> structure -> verify -> score -> signal -> trade) and errors in early stages cascade. If the LLM extracts statements incorrectly (e.g., misclassifies a historical statement as forward-looking, or fails to extract a real forward-looking statement), the credibility score is corrupted. If the verification matching fails (can't map the call metric to the 10-Q line item due to segment restructuring or accounting changes), true accuracy goes unmeasured. The signal quality is bounded by extraction quality, and extraction quality varies across industries (tech companies give different types of guidance than banks or retailers). Second, the signal requires a substantial lookback period to build credibility histories -- new IPO management teams have no score, and the signal is silent on them. Third, management teams may game credibility scores once the metric is known (give specific guidance on easily controllable metrics, vague guidance on risky ones), which would inflate CS without improving information content. Fourth, the signal is long-biased (more positive guidance than negative guidance from management), which means the alpha may come primarily from avoiding losers rather than picking winners -- useful but less exciting.

## Summary Cross-Reference

| Dimension | H1: Q&A Coherence Decay | H2: Analyst Question Cartel | H3: Management Credibility Trajectory |
|-----------|------------------------|-----------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Signal source** | Within-call speech trajectory | Cross-analyst question similarity | Multi-event statement accuracy |
| **Predicts** | Future earnings surprises | Short-term mean reversion | Post-guidance drift |
| **Holding period** | 1-6 months | 1-4 weeks | 1-6 months |
| **Data** | Transcripts + estimates | Transcripts + prices | Transcripts + SEC filings + estimates |
| **LLM role** | Embedding similarity trajectory | Pairwise similarity matrix | Extraction + cross-document matching |
| **Direction** | Short-biased | Contrarian (both sides) | Long-biased |
| **Key risk** | Management adaptation | Endogeneity of consensus | Pipeline complexity / error cascade |
| **Confidence** | Medium | Medium-Low | Medium-High |

These three hypotheses collectively probe the narrative ecosystem at three different temporal scales and three different levels of analysis: within-event (single call dynamics), cross-analyst (consensus homogeneity), and multi-event (credibility trajectory over time). They share a common thread -- LLMs transform what was previously qualitative, unscalable narrative analysis into quantitative, systematic signals -- but exploit different market inefficiencies and require different holding periods, providing diversification across idea types.
