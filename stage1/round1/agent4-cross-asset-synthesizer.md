# Agent 4: Cross-Asset Synthesizer — Round 1 Hypotheses

Agent Belief: Information arrives in one market first and propagates with measurable delay; LLMs can monitor multiple asset classes simultaneously to detect these propagation lags at retail scale for the first time.

---

## HYPOTHESIS 1: Supply Chain Shock Transmission via 10-K Customer/Supplier Relationship Extraction

**HYPOTHESIS NAME:** Supply Chain Shock Transmission via 10-K Relationship Extraction

**SOURCE AGENT:** Cross-Asset Synthesizer

**MECHANISM:** When a publicly traded U.S. company (the "customer") reports a large negative earnings surprise or guidance cut, its publicly traded U.S. suppliers experience negative equity returns with a measurable delay of 1-5 trading days because: (1) the market does not immediately trace supply chain implications beyond obvious tier-1 relationships (e.g., Apple/Foxconn), (2) supplier dependency is disclosed in unstructured 10-K footnotes, Item 1 business descriptions, and ASC 280 segment reporting — not in any machine-readable database, and (3) the revenue-at-risk magnitude varies by supplier based on customer concentration percentages disclosed exclusively in narrative text. The causal chain is: customer fundamental shock —> reduced procurement from supplier —> supplier future revenue impairment —> supplier equity underperformance. The lag exists because analysts update supplier estimates only after their attention is drawn to the issue, and the relationship is not surfaced by traditional data vendors in real time.

**LLM ADVANTAGE:** An LLM uniquely enables three things that no existing automated system does: (1) **Relationship Extraction from Unstructured Text:** Traditional quant models identify supply chain links using coarse industry classifications (SIC/NAICS co-occurrence) or commercial databases that cover only large/obvious relationships. An LLM reads every 10-K in the investment universe and extracts specific customer/supplier relationships from narrative disclosures — including sentences like "Sales to ABC Corp accounted for 18% of consolidated revenue in fiscal 2024" (Item 1), or "We depend on a single supplier for our primary raw material" (Item 1A risk factors). (2) **Concentration Quantification:** The LLM extracts the exact percentage of revenue or cost dependency, enabling a dollar-denominated estimate of revenue-at-risk rather than a binary "linked/not-linked" flag. (3) **Directional Filtering:** The LLM classifies whether the relationship is upstream (supplier to the shocked company), downstream (customer of the shocked company), or horizontal (competitor), filtering to the correct direction of shock propagation. A rules-based NLP system would fail because disclosure language varies enormously across companies — no regex captures "our largest customer, representing approximately 22% of net revenue" AND "three customers individually account for more than 10% of sales: Customer A (14%), Customer B (12%), and Customer C (11%)."

**WHY UNDERWEIGHTED:** (1) **Too cross-domain:** This requires simultaneously monitoring earnings events across 3,000+ stocks, then cross-referencing each shock against a directed dependency graph of 10,000+ relationships extracted from EDGAR filings. No human analyst team can do this in real time. (2) **Data not machine-readable:** The customer/supplier links are buried in 10-K narrative text. The SEC does not require structured tagging of these disclosures beyond basic segment reporting. Commercial supply chain databases (Bloomberg SPLC, FactSet Supply Chain) cover primarily large-cap, widely-followed relationships and miss the mid-cap and small-cap links where the propagation lag is largest. (3) **Academic work exists but is stale:** Cohen & Frazzini (2008) demonstrated customer-supplier return predictability using Compustat segment data, but their method identifies relationships only through standardized segment disclosures (which many companies avoid via aggregation) and cannot read the footnotes. Their signal has subsequently decayed in large caps while persisting in the mid/small-cap universe where data coverage is poorest — exactly where LLM extraction from raw text provides the most value. (4) **No real-time monitoring product exists for retail:** Institutional funds have internal systems for this, but no retail-accessible tool monitors EDGAR filings, builds a live dependency graph, and fires alerts on earnings shock propagation.

**HOLDING PERIOD:** 1-5 days (the propagation window from customer shock to supplier equity repricing)

**DATA REQUIREMENT:**
- **SEC EDGAR 10-K filings** (free, sec.gov or EDGAR API): Full text of Item 1 (Business), Item 1A (Risk Factors), Item 7 (MD&A), and Notes to Financial Statements (Segment Reporting and Major Customer disclosures). Filing date (not period end date) used for temporal alignment. Coverage: all US-listed companies, 2000-present.
- **SEC EDGAR 8-K filings** (free): For earnings releases and guidance updates, Item 2.02 (Results of Operations) and Item 7.01 (Regulation FD Disclosure). Filing date used as event date.
- **Equity price data** (Yahoo Finance, free EOD): Daily close prices for all US-listed equities. Market cap data for size matching.
- **Industry classification** (free, SIC or NAICS from SEC EDGAR or Yahoo Finance): For sector-matching control groups.
- **Minimum universe:** Russell 3000 constituents (point-in-time, survivorship-free from FMP free tier or reconstructed from EDGAR listing data).
- **Time period for backtest:** 2015-2025 for out-of-sample validation; 2005-2014 for robustness check.

**PROCESSING APPROACH:**

Step 1 — **Static Dependency Graph Construction (run quarterly, reusing latest 10-K for each stock):**
- Feed each 10-K filing to an LLM (temperature=0, deterministic) with a structured extraction prompt: "Extract all customer relationships where this company is a supplier. For each, output: customer name (ticker if possible), revenue percentage, and whether this is characterized as a 'major customer' or 'significant dependency.' Also extract all supplier dependencies: supplier name, input/cost percentage, and whether characterized as 'sole source' or 'critical supplier.'"
- Output: structured JSON per filing. Map company names to tickers using a name-to-ticker resolution step (LLM-assisted fuzzy matching with manual verification for high-value targets).
- Build a directed graph: nodes = tickers, edges = "A supplies to B" with weight = revenue percentage.

Step 2 — **Real-Time Event Monitoring (daily):**
- Each trading day, scan new 8-K filings for earnings releases and guidance updates.
- For each release, compute earnings surprise vs. consensus (revenue surprise % and EPS surprise %). Consensus data from FMP or Yahoo Finance analyst estimates (free tier).
- Flag "shock events": revenue surprise < -3% AND/OR EPS surprise < -5% AND/OR guidance cut (LLM classifies the 8-K text for guidance language: "lowered," "reduced," "below prior expectations," "revised downward").
- For each shock event, traverse the dependency graph to find all supplier nodes.
- For each supplier, execute Step 3.

Step 3 — **Revenue-at-Risk Estimation (per shock event):**
- Feed the supplier's most recent 10-K to the LLM with a targeted prompt: "This company disclosed that [customer name] represents [X]% of revenue. The customer just reported a [Y]% revenue miss and guided [Z]% lower. Based on the supplier's disclosures about this relationship in the 10-K, estimate the revenue-at-risk for the supplier over the next 2-4 quarters. Consider: (a) the customer concentration percentage, (b) whether the relationship appears exclusive or one-of-many, and (c) whether the supplier's risk factor language discusses customer loss risk explicitly."
- Output: "Revenue-at-risk score" on a 0-10 scale and an estimated percentage of supplier revenue (e.g., "2.5% of annual revenue at risk").
- Filter to suppliers with revenue-at-risk >= 2% of their own annual revenue.

Step 4 — **Trade Construction:**
- For each qualifying supplier, enter a short position (or avoid/buy puts if short-selling is constrained) sized proportionally to the revenue-at-risk score.
- Hold for 5 trading days.
- Exit on day 5 or earlier if the supplier's stock price decline reaches the estimated revenue-at-risk percentage (profit-taking).

**FALSIFIABLE PREDICTION:** If this edge is real, a portfolio of short positions in qualified suppliers — entered at the close on the day of the customer's shock announcement and held for 5 trading days — should generate an average excess return of at least -80 bps (negative 80 basis points) relative to sector-and-size-matched control stocks over the 5-day window. The effect should be strongest in the mid-cap ($2B-$10B market cap) and small-cap ($300M-$2B) segments where analyst coverage is thin. The effect should be weakest (near zero) for large-cap suppliers ($10B+ market cap, >15 analysts) where the relationship is already widely known.

If this edge is fake, we would observe: (a) no statistically significant difference between supplier returns and sector-size-matched controls over the 1-5 day window after controlling for the customer stock's own contemporaneous return (i.e., the supplier is moving with sector/customer co-movement, not with an additional shock propagation lag), or (b) any apparent excess return is fully explained by common factor exposures (market beta, size, value, momentum).

**Specific quantitative bounds:**
- Real: Mean excess return of supplier cohort <= -80 bps over days 1-5, p < 0.01 after FDR correction, monotonic with revenue-at-risk score, and robust to exclusion of the top/bottom 5% of observations.
- Fake: Mean excess return > -30 bps, not statistically distinguishable from zero at p < 0.05, or disappears when top 5% of observations are excluded.

**MINIMUM EFFECT SIZE:** Average excess return of at least -80 bps per event (5-day window), corresponding to post-cost alpha of approximately -50 to -60 bps after estimated transaction costs of 20-30 bps round-trip (bid-ask spread for mid-cap stocks, zero commissions). At 30-50 qualifying shock events per year and 5-10 concurrent positions of $5,000-$10,000 each, this generates approximately $1,500-$4,000/year in expected profit on $100K capital (1.5-4% annual return contribution from a strategy that is invested only ~40-60% of trading days). Below this threshold, the strategy does not justify the monitoring and execution time cost for a retail trader.

**OUT-OF-SAMPLE PLAN:** (1) Temporal split: train the dependency graph extraction methodology and shock classification on 2015-2020 data, validate on 2021-2025 data. (2) Cross-sectional robustness: test separately on large-cap (>$10B), mid-cap ($2B-$10B), and small-cap ($300M-$2B) universes — the hypothesis predicts the effect is strongest in mid/small-cap. (3) Alternative data source: test whether the effect disappears when using only machine-readable Compustat segment data (the Cohen-Frazzini method) vs. the full-text LLM extraction — the hypothesis predicts the LLM extraction identifies additional relationships that generate incremental alpha. (4) False positive control: randomly reassign customer-supplier links (preserving the network structure but scrambling the identities) and verify the alpha vanishes — this tests whether it's the specific relationships rather than network centrality that drives returns.

**SELF-ASSESSED CONFIDENCE:** Medium. The customer-supplier return predictability mechanism is well-established in academic literature (Cohen & Frazzini 2008, Menzly & Ozbas 2010), which reduces the risk of a completely spurious claim. However, the specific contributions of this hypothesis — (a) that LLM extraction from footnote text captures relationships missed by standardized segment data, and (b) that the effect persists in the mid/small-cap universe where relationships are less obvious — must be empirically validated. The risk is that the marginal relationships discovered by the LLM (beyond what's in structured segment data) are too small or too ambiguous to generate tradable signals.

**BIGGEST WEAKNESS:** Name-to-ticker resolution is the practical Achilles' heel. 10-K disclosures often reference customer names in abbreviated or colloquial form ("ABC Corp" vs. "ABC Corporation" vs. "ABC Industries Inc."), and some major customers are private companies without tickers (e.g., "Cargill" or "Mars Inc."). If the LLM cannot reliably map disclosed customer names to public tickers, the dependency graph will have coverage gaps that introduce selection bias (only the easiest-to-resolve relationships are included, which are likely the most widely-known and least alpha-generating). A related concern: private company customers are excluded from the signal even though they could have public suppliers, reducing the opportunity set. The backtest must explicitly measure the relationship resolution rate and test whether signal strength varies by resolution confidence.

## HYPOTHESIS 2: Commodity Input Cost Transmission Delay via 10-K Sensitivity Language

**HYPOTHESIS NAME:** Commodity Cost Transmission Delay via 10-K Sensitivity Extraction

**SOURCE AGENT:** Cross-Asset Synthesizer

**MECHANISM:** When a major commodity price (crude oil, natural gas, copper, steel, corn, etc.) moves by more than 5% in a week, companies that are significant consumers of that commodity — as disclosed in their 10-K "quantitative and qualitative disclosures about market risk" (Item 7A) and risk factor sections (Item 1A) — experience equity price adjustments with a delay of 2-10 trading days because: (1) traders monitoring commodity markets are typically commodity specialists who do not cross-reference commodity moves with individual equity 10-K sensitivity disclosures, (2) equity analysts update their earnings models for input cost changes only during scheduled model refreshes (quarterly, or when the commodity move is extreme), and (3) the exact dollar impact of a commodity price change on a specific company's earnings requires reading the company's own sensitivity disclosure — a number buried in a paragraph of text that varies in format across companies. The causal chain is: commodity spot/futures price change —> company input cost change —> gross margin compression/expansion —> earnings surprise relative to stale analyst estimates —> equity price adjustment. The delay is the time between the commodity price signal and the equity market's incorporation of the revised earnings outlook.

**LLM ADVANTAGE:** An LLM does three things that existing approaches cannot: (1) **Precise Sensitivity Parameter Extraction:** Companies disclose commodity sensitivity in idiosyncratic language: "A $1.00 increase in the average price of a barrel of crude oil would increase our annual fuel expense by approximately $45 million" (airline 10-K), or "A 10% increase in aluminum raw material costs would reduce our gross profit by approximately $15 million annually" (packaging company 10-K). An LLM extracts the exact dollar sensitivity and the reference commodity from this unstructured text — a regex approach fails because the disclosure format varies across thousands of companies. (2) **Real-Time Dollar Impact Computation:** Once the sensitivity parameter is extracted, the LLM can compute the real-time dollar impact of a commodity price change on each company: "West Texas Intermediate crude increased $5.42/bbl this week; based on your 10-K sensitivity disclosure of $45M per $1/bbl, your annual fuel expense is now expected to increase by approximately $244M, representing a 3.2% headwind to operating margin." This computation requires both numerical reasoning (applying the sensitivity parameter to the observed price change) and contextualization (expressing the impact relative to the company's financials). (3) **Supply Chain Amplification Detection:** The LLM reads beyond the direct commodity disclosure to detect whether the company also has indirect commodity exposure — e.g., "our suppliers face similar raw material cost pressures, which could be passed through to us" (risk factor language). This indirect exposure is invisible to quantitative commodity beta models.

**WHY UNDERWEIGHTED:** (1) **Requires simultaneous monitoring of 30+ commodities cross-referenced against 3,000+ company-specific sensitivity disclosures.** No human can track this in real time. Institutional quant funds use industry-level commodity betas (e.g., "energy sector = 0.6 beta to oil"), which work at the sector level but wash out at the individual stock level for diversified companies with atypical commodity exposures. (2) **Sensitivity disclosures are in unstructured 10-K text.** There is no structured, machine-readable database of "Company X is exposed to Commodity Y with sensitivity Z." Building one requires reading every 10-K. Before LLMs, this required costly manual data entry by human analysts — which is why commercial data vendors (Bloomberg, FactSet) cover this for only the largest 500-1,000 companies and only for major commodities (oil, gas, copper). (3) **Commodity-equity cross-asset arbitrage is fragmented across trading desks.** Commodity traders trade commodities; equity traders trade equities. Neither systematically cross-references the other's domain at the individual company level. This structural separation of expertise creates a propagation delay. (4) **The signal is event-driven and episodic, not continuous.** It only "fires" when commodity prices make large moves, meaning it's not worth building institutional infrastructure around unless you already have the LLM capability. For retail, the infrequency is actually an advantage — the trader is not competing with HFT firms on speed, and the infrequent events fit a "check once per day" workflow.

**HOLDING PERIOD:** 2-10 days (the typical lag between a large commodity move and equity analyst model updates)

**DATA REQUIREMENT:**
- **Commodity futures prices** (Yahoo Finance, free EOD, or FRED, free): Daily settlement prices for front-month futures: WTI crude oil (CL=F), Henry Hub natural gas (NG=F), copper (HG=F), gold (GC=F), corn (ZC=F), soybeans (ZS=F), wheat (ZW=F), steel (via hot-rolled coil or SLX ETF as proxy), aluminum (via JJU ETF or LME proxy). Minimum: top 10 commodities by total US corporate input cost exposure.
- **SEC EDGAR 10-K filings** (free): Item 7A (Quantitative and Qualitative Disclosures About Market Risk) for commodity sensitivity language, Item 1A (Risk Factors) for raw material cost risk, and Item 7 (MD&A) for cost of goods sold breakdown.
- **Equity price data** (Yahoo Finance, free EOD): Daily close prices for all US-listed equities. Market cap and sector classification.
- **Analyst earnings estimates** (Financial Modeling Prep free tier, or Yahoo Finance analyst estimates): Consensus EPS for next quarter, to measure whether the commodity-driven earnings impact is already reflected.
- **Time period:** 2010-2025. Commodity price volatility was elevated 2010-2015 and 2020-2023, providing sufficient commodity shock events for testing.

**PROCESSING APPROACH:**

Step 1 — **Commodity Sensitivity Database Construction (quarterly, from latest 10-K):**
- For each stock in the universe, feed Item 7A and Item 1A sections of the 10-K to an LLM (temperature=0) with a structured extraction prompt: "Extract all commodity sensitivity disclosures. For each commodity mentioned, output: (a) the commodity, (b) the direction of exposure (input cost, revenue sensitivity, or hedging relationship), (c) the quantitative sensitivity if disclosed (e.g., '$X million per $Y/unit change'), (d) the base price or reference year, and (e) whether the company describes a formal hedging program for this commodity."
- Output: structured JSON with sensitivity parameters for each commodity-company pair.
- Filter: retain only "input cost" exposures (companies that consume the commodity as a production input, where price increases are negative for earnings). Remove companies with comprehensive hedging programs (>80% hedged) as the commodity price change does not flow through to earnings. Also remove commodity producers (e.g., oil companies with positive oil exposure) — these are not about input cost transmission.

Step 2 — **Commodity Shock Detection (daily):**
- Each trading day, compute the 5-trading-day rolling return for each tracked commodity.
- Flag "shock events" when the 5-day return exceeds 2 standard deviations of the commodity's own 1-year rolling distribution (approximately >5-8% for most commodities, adjusting for each commodity's volatility).
- Tag each shock event by magnitude and direction: "Crude oil +7.3% over 5 days" or "Natural gas -12.1% over 5 days."

Step 3 — **Impact Scoring (per shock event):**
- For each commodity shock event, query the sensitivity database for all companies with input cost exposure to that commodity.
- For each exposed company, compute a "dollar impact score": extracted_sensitivity ($ per commodity unit) * observed price change (commodity units) / trailing annual operating income. This gives the expected operating income impact as a percentage.
- Compute a "stale estimate score": the difference between the LLM-computed earnings impact and any analyst estimate revisions over the past 5 days (from Yahoo Finance or FMP). If analysts have already revised estimates in response to the commodity move, the signal is stale — discard.
- Retain only companies where: (a) dollar impact score > 1% of operating income, and (b) fewer than 2 analysts have revised estimates in the past 5 days.

Step 4 — **Trade Construction:**
- Commodity price increase + input cost exposure = negative for company earnings = enter short position.
- Commodity price decrease + input cost exposure = positive for company earnings = enter long position.
- Position size proportional to dollar impact score.
- Hold for 5 trading days (exit on day 5 or when an equity analyst revises estimates for the company, whichever comes first — the analyst revision is the catalyst that closes the propagation gap).

**FALSIFIABLE PREDICTION:** If this edge is real, a portfolio that goes long companies benefiting from commodity input cost declines and short companies harmed by commodity input cost increases — with positions entered 1 day after the commodity shock is identified and held for 5 trading days — should generate an annualized alpha of >5% (Sharpe > 0.5) with low correlation to the commodity itself (commodity beta < 0.3). The effect should be stronger in companies with: (a) no formal hedging program, (b) fewer than 10 analyst covering, (c) commodity exposure buried in risk factors rather than quantified in Item 7A, and (d) market cap below $5B.

If this edge is fake, we would observe: (a) the alpha is near zero after netting out the contemporaneous commodity price move (i.e., the equity moves are just a proxy for commodity beta, not a delayed transmission effect), or (b) the alpha disappears when controlling for the company's industry (the effect is just sector-level commodity beta, not company-specific sensitivity from 10-K language), or (c) there is no monotonic relationship between the LLM-extracted sensitivity magnitude and the equity return magnitude.

**Specific quantitative bounds:**
- Real: Long-short portfolio (quintile 5 = highest negative impact vs. quintile 1 = highest positive impact) shows annualized alpha >5% with t-statistic >2.5 after controlling for Fama-French 5-factor + momentum. Information coefficient (IC) > 0.04 between dollar impact score and subsequent 5-day return. Monotonic across impact quintiles.
- Fake: Alpha <2% annualized, t-statistic <1.5, IC < 0.01, no monotonicity across quintiles, or alpha decays to zero within the first 2 years of the out-of-sample period.

**MINIMUM EFFECT SIZE:** Annualized long-short alpha of at least 5% after transaction costs (estimated 3-4% annualized for the long-short portfolio assuming 20-30 bps round-trip per trade and ~150 trades per year). This corresponds to an expected annual profit of $2,500-$5,000 on $100K capital assuming 50% of capital is deployed in this strategy. Below 5% annualized alpha, the strategy is not worth the monitoring burden (daily commodity price checks + quarterly 10-K re-processing) compared to simply holding a diversified equity portfolio.

**OUT-OF-SAMPLE PLAN:** (1) Temporal split: calibrate sensitivity extraction and impact-scoring methodology on 2010-2019 data, validate on 2020-2025 data. The 2020-2025 period includes the COVID commodity shock (negative oil prices April 2020), the 2021-2022 commodity inflation surge (natural gas spike, grain price spikes after Russia-Ukraine), and the 2023-2025 disinflation — a wide range of commodity regimes. (2) Commodity-by-commodity test: measure the alpha separately for each commodity. The hypothesis predicts stronger alpha for commodities without liquid futures markets (where commodity traders are fewer and propagation is slower — e.g., steel/hot-rolled coil) and weaker for the most liquid commodities (crude oil, gold) where propagation should be fastest. (3) Hedging filter robustness: test whether removing companies with disclosed hedging programs (per LLM extraction) significantly improves the alpha vs. the full sample. (4) Sector neutralization: construct a sector-neutral version of the long-short portfolio and verify the alpha survives — if the signal is entirely sector-level (e.g., "airlines are down because oil went up"), the alpha disappears after sector neutralization.

**SELF-ASSESSED CONFIDENCE:** Medium. The causal mechanism (input costs matter for earnings, earnings matter for stock prices, analysts are slow) is economically uncontroversial. The unknown is the transmission delay: do equity market makers and algorithmic traders already incorporate commodity price changes into equity prices within minutes/hours, making any signal stale by the time a daily-frequency retail trader can act? Empirically, commodity-equity correlation has increased over time (the "financialization of commodities"), which could mean the transmission delay has shrunk to intraday. However, the company-SPECIFIC aspect (via 10-K sensitivity language, not sector beta) may still provide alpha because the company-level mapping is not automated. The biggest empirical risk is that the signal works only for a handful of obvious relationships (airlines-oil, chemicals-natgas) that are already in institutional models, and provides no incremental value for diversified companies with small commodity exposures.

**BIGGEST WEAKNESS:** **Look-back window bias in sensitivity extraction.** 10-K sensitivity disclosures are static snapshots — they reflect the company's commodity exposure at the prior fiscal year-end. If a company changes its commodity consumption mix or hedging program mid-year, the LLM-extracted sensitivity parameter from the 10-K becomes stale until the next filing. This is particularly acute for commodity shocks that occur late in a company's fiscal year (months 9-12 after the last 10-K). Example: an airline that significantly increased fuel hedging after the 2022 oil spike would have a 10-K that no longer reflects its true exposure by late 2023. The backtest must track the EXACT filing date of each 10-K and measure the decay in signal strength as the filing ages. If signal strength is concentrated in the first 3 months after each 10-K filing and decays to zero by month 6, then the strategy's opportunity window is too narrow for a retail trader checking once per day — they'd need to re-process 10-Ks far more frequently, which is infeasible.

## HYPOTHESIS 3: Post-FOMC Bond-Equity Divergence Resolution via Statement Language Classification

**HYPOTHESIS NAME:** Post-FOMC Divergence Resolution via Statement Concern Classification

**SOURCE AGENT:** Cross-Asset Synthesizer

**MECHANISM:** After Federal Reserve FOMC decisions, the bond market (2-year and 10-year Treasury yields) and the equity market (S&P 500 sector ETFs) sometimes react in opposite directions: bonds rally (yields fall) while equities sell off, or bonds sell off (yields rise) while equities rally. These "divergence events" occur because the bond market interprets the FOMC statement through one lens (inflation expectations, rate path) while the equity market interprets it through another (growth outlook, discount rate). When the two markets diverge, one market's interpretation will prove correct and "resolve" over the subsequent 5-15 trading days as the dominant macro narrative crystallizes. An LLM, by reading the FOMC statement and the Chair's press conference transcript, can classify the Federal Reserve's **primary concern** (inflation, growth, labor market, or financial stability) and determine which market's reaction is consistent with the Fed's actual message — and therefore which market's initial move will likely reverse. The causal chain is: FOMC statement —> divergent bond/equity reactions —> LLM classifies primary concern and prevailing interpretation in each market —> identifies the "misaligned" market —> trades the reversal in sector ETFs.

**Specific example of the mechanism:** On a given FOMC day, the S&P 500 rallies +1.2% (interpreting the statement as "dovish — rate cuts coming soon") while the 2-year yield also rises +8 bps (interpreting the statement as "hawkish — inflation risk means rates stay higher for longer"). This is a divergence. If the LLM classifies the statement's primary concern as "inflation" (detecting language like "inflation remains elevated," "the Committee is highly attentive to inflation risks," "further progress toward the 2% objective is not assured"), then the bond market's interpretation (hawkish, yields higher) is consistent with the Fed's actual message, while the equity market's interpretation (dovish, rally) is not. The equity rally should reverse over the next 5-10 days as the growth implications of persistent inflation are absorbed. Conversely, if the LLM classifies the primary concern as "growth" ("the pace of economic activity has moderated," "risks to the outlook have become more balanced"), the equity market's negative reaction is the correct one and bonds are overreacting.

**LLM ADVANTAGE:** Three capabilities that no existing quantitative model provides: (1) **Multi-Label Concern Classification from Nuanced Central Bank Language:** The FOMC statement contains carefully calibrated language where subtle shifts in wording signal changes in the Committee's priorities. For example, the change from "the Committee is highly attentive to inflation risks" (September 2023) to "the Committee judges that the risks to achieving its employment and inflation goals are moving into better balance" (December 2023) signaled a pivot from inflation-first to balanced-concerns — a shift that a traditional keyword-count NLP model would miss because both sentences contain "inflation" and "risks." An LLM classifies the statement into a probability distribution over concern categories (inflation, growth, labor, financial stability) and detects directional shifts from the prior meeting. (2) **Press Conference Q&A Interpretation:** The Chair's answers to reporter questions during the press conference often contain more candid language than the carefully-negotiated statement. When a reporter asks "Are you worried about a recession?" and the Chair responds "We don't see recession risks as elevated at this time, but we are monitoring the pace of consumer spending carefully," an LLM detects the implicit acknowledgment of growth concern despite the "no recession" headline. A rules-based sentiment model captures only the headline. (3) **Cross-Market Narrative Consistency Check:** The LLM cross-references the Fed's concern classification against the direction of bond and equity moves to perform a "consistency check" — does this market's reaction match the Fed's message? This requires simultaneous reasoning about: the Fed's stated concern, the economic implication of that concern for equity sectors (inflation concern = negative for high-growth/low-profit tech, positive for energy/financials; growth concern = negative for cyclicals, positive for defensives), and the rate-path implication for bonds. This multi-hop reasoning from text to asset class implication is something only an LLM can do without hard-coded rules.

**WHY UNDERWEIGHTED:** (1) **The consensus view is that post-FOMC moves are noise, not signal.** The academic literature finds that FOMC days have elevated volatility but no systematic post-FOMC drift — the market reaction is assumed to be efficient. This hypothesis challenges that by saying: the AVERAGE post-FOMC drift is zero, but CONDITIONAL on divergence + LLM concern classification, there is a predictable reversal. No prior work has tested this conditional hypothesis. (2) **Requires cross-asset reasoning that is organizationally rare in the investment industry.** Bond fund managers analyze the FOMC statement for rate implications; equity fund managers analyze it for growth implications. Neither systematically checks whether the OTHER market's reaction is consistent. The LLM bridges this organizational gap at zero marginal cost. (3) **The time window is awkward for institutional funds.** FOMC events happen 8 times per year, generating maybe 4-6 divergence events annually. Most institutional strategies require more frequent signals to justify dedicated capital allocation. For a retail trader, 4-6 events per year is entirely manageable — they wait for the signal, size appropriately, and otherwise stay in passive investments. (4) **The signal requires reading the full statement and press conference transcript, not just the numeric decision.** Algorithmic trading systems parse the rate decision number in milliseconds but cannot parse the nuanced linguistic content of the statement and press conference. The human analysts who CAN parse it are not systematically cross-referencing bond and equity reactions in real time.

**HOLDING PERIOD:** 5-15 trading days (the typical window for the divergent interpretation to resolve)

**DATA REQUIREMENT:**
- **FOMC statements** (Federal Reserve website, federalreserve.gov, free): Full text of each FOMC statement, released at 2:00 PM ET on decision days. Available since 1994. Archived and timestamped.
- **FOMC press conference transcripts** (Federal Reserve website, free): Full transcript of the Chair's opening remarks and Q&A session, released same day (typically available within 2-3 hours of the press conference). Available since 2011 (when post-meeting press conferences began).
- **Treasury yield data** (FRED, free, or Yahoo Finance for ^TNX, ^IRX): Daily closing yields for 2-year, 10-year, and 30-year Treasury securities. Intraday data not required (daily close is sufficient to measure the direction of the bond market's FOMC-day reaction).
- **Sector ETF price data** (Yahoo Finance, free EOD): Daily closing prices for the 11 S&P 500 sector ETFs (XLE, XLF, XLK, XLV, XLI, XLP, XLY, XLU, XLB, XLRE, XLC). Also S&P 500 (SPY) for market-level reaction.
- **FOMC calendar** (Federal Reserve website, free): Scheduled meeting dates for event identification.
- **Time period:** 2011-2025 (post-press-conference era). 8 meetings/year * 15 years = ~120 FOMC events. Estimated divergence events: ~40-60 (roughly 30-50% of meetings, based on prior work finding divergent bond-equity reactions on approximately 40% of FOMC days).

**PROCESSING APPROACH:**

Step 1 — **FOMC Statement and Press Conference Classification (per event):**
- Feed the FOMC statement text to an LLM (temperature=0) with a structured classification prompt: "Classify the FOMC statement by the Committee's PRIMARY concern. Output a distribution over four categories: (1) Inflation — the Committee is primarily worried about inflation staying above target, (2) Growth — the Committee is primarily worried about economic slowdown or recession, (3) Labor Market — the Committee is primarily focused on employment conditions (strength or weakness), (4) Financial Stability — the Committee is primarily concerned about financial system risks. Also classify the DIRECTION OF SHIFT from the prior statement: Hawkish, Dovish, or Neutral."
- Feed the press conference transcript to the LLM with a separate prompt: "Classify whether the Chair's answers in Q&A reveal concerns NOT emphasized in the formal statement. Specifically: does the Chair express more concern about inflation, growth, labor, or financial stability than the statement suggests? Output: (a) the dominant 'Q&A concern' category, (b) a 'concern gap' score of 0-10 indicating how much the Q&A concern differs from the statement concern, and (c) specific quotes supporting this assessment."
- Output: Concern classification (statement), concern classification (Q&A), hawkish/dovish direction, concern gap score.

Step 2 — **Divergence Detection (per event):**
- Measure FOMC-day return: S&P 500 (SPY) close-to-close return on the FOMC day.
- Measure FOMC-day bond move: change in 10-year yield (in basis points) on the FOMC day.
- Define a "divergence event" as: SPY return > +0.5% AND 10Y yield change > +5 bps (both up — equities rallying on "dovish," bonds selling off — equities disagreeing with bonds on rate direction), OR SPY return < -0.5% AND 10Y yield change < -5 bps (both down — equities selling off, bonds rallying — again disagreeing on direction). These thresholds filter out the ~60% of FOMC days where equities and bonds move in the same direction (both up on dovish surprise, both down on hawkish surprise).
- On non-divergence days: no signal, no trade.

Step 3 — **Consistency Check (per divergence event):**
- The LLM performs a multi-hop reasoning step:
  - Given: FOMC primary concern = [category], hawkish/dovish direction = [direction].
  - Check 1: Is the BOND market's reaction consistent? If inflation concern + hawkish —> bonds selling off (yields higher) is consistent. If growth concern + dovish —> bonds rallying (yields lower) is consistent. If the bond reaction is INCONSISTENT with the concern+direction, flag bond reaction as "likely to reverse."
  - Check 2: Is the EQUITY market's reaction consistent? If growth concern + dovish —> equity rally is partially justified (lower rates = higher valuations) but the growth concern itself is negative for earnings —> the net effect depends on whether rates or growth dominates. The LLM weighs these based on the statement's language emphasis.
  - Output: "Resolution direction" — which market will "win," and therefore which sectors to long/short.
  - Example outputs:
    - "Inflation concern + hawkish, bond sell-off is CONSISTENT, equity rally is INCONSISTENT. Resolution: equities reverse lower, favoring defensives (XLP, XLU) over cyclicals (XLY, XLI)."
    - "Growth concern + dovish, bond rally is CONSISTENT, equity sell-off is INCONSISTENT (growth concern was already priced, dovish signal is new information). Resolution: equities reverse higher, favoring growth (XLK, XLC) over value (XLE, XLF)."

Step 4 — **Trade Construction:**
- On the trading day AFTER the FOMC decision (T+1), enter a long-short sector ETF pair trade based on the resolution direction.
- Position: Long the sector(s) favored by the resolution, short the sector(s) disfavored. Dollar-neutral.
- Hold for 10 trading days (through T+11).
- Exit on day 10 or earlier if the divergence closes (SPY and 10Y yield resume moving in the same direction).

**FALSIFIABLE PREDICTION:** If this edge is real, a long-short sector ETF pair trade entered at the close on T+1 (the day after an FOMC divergence event with LLM-classified resolution direction) and held for 10 trading days should generate:
- (a) A win rate >65% (the pair trade moves in the predicted direction) vs. a random baseline of ~50%.
- (b) An average return per trade >1.5% (gross of costs) vs. a simple "always fade the equity move" strategy which should average near zero.
- (c) The return should be strongest when the LLM-assigned "concern gap score" (Step 1, the difference between statement and Q&A emphasis) is high (>7/10), indicating that the press conference revealed additional information not in the statement.

If this edge is fake, we would observe:
- (a) The win rate is indistinguishable from 50% at p < 0.05.
- (b) The average return per trade is not statistically different from zero.
- (c) The "concern gap" conditioning provides no incremental discrimination — trades with high vs. low concern gap scores perform identically.
- (d) A simple quantitative rule ("fade the equity market move on all divergence days, regardless of LLM classification") performs just as well as the LLM-guided version — proving the LLM adds no value.

**Specific quantitative bounds:**
- Real: Win rate 65-75% on 40-60 historical divergence events (2011-2025). Average return per trade >1.5% gross. Sharpe ratio >1.0 on the strategy (since it's deployed only ~5% of trading days, Sharpe is computed on the time series of trade returns). p < 0.01 on a bootstrap test that shuffles the resolution direction labels.
- Fake: Win rate 45-55%, average return -0.5% to +0.5%, p > 0.10 on label shuffling test, or LLM-guided version does not outperform the simple "fade equity" rule by >30 bps per trade.

**MINIMUM EFFECT SIZE:** Average return per trade of at least 1.0% gross (approximately 0.8% net after ETF bid-ask spreads of ~5-10 bps per leg, total round-trip costs ~20-40 bps). At 4-6 trades per year with 10% of capital allocated per trade ($10,000 on $100K capital), this generates $320-$480/year in expected profit after costs. While this absolute dollar return is modest, the strategy requires only ~50 trading days of active exposure per year and the rest of capital can be in passive investments. The key metric is: can the strategy generate a positive expected value with a win rate statistically distinguishable from random? A 1.0% gross return per trade on a 65% win rate is the minimum threshold because below this, a single large loss (the 35% of losing trades) could wipe out multiple winning trades, making the strategy's equity curve too volatile for a retail trader to stick with.

**OUT-OF-SAMPLE PLAN:** (1) Temporal split: train the LLM classification methodology and consistency-check logic on 2011-2019 FOMC events (covering Yellen and early Powell eras), validate on 2020-2025 events (covering COVID-era emergency actions, the 2022-2023 hiking cycle, and 2024-2025 easing). This is a stringent test because the macro regime changed dramatically: the 2011-2019 period was predominantly low-rate/low-inflation, while 2020-2025 saw the most aggressive hiking cycle in 40 years followed by an easing transition. If the LLM classification works across both regimes, it's genuinely detecting the Fed's concern rather than overfitting to a single regime's language patterns. (2) Central bank generalization test: apply the same methodology to ECB and Bank of England decisions for European and UK equity markets. The hypothesis predicts the mechanism is general to any central bank where the statement contains nuanced concern language. If it fails outside the Fed, the LLM may have overfit to Fedspeak patterns. (3) False positive test: apply the LLM consistency check to randomly selected NON-FOMC days with large bond-equity divergences (e.g., CPI release days, NFP days). The hypothesis predicts the resolution logic only works for FOMC days because other drivers of bond-equity divergence (growth scares, geopolitical events) have different resolution mechanisms. If the LLM predicts resolution correctly on non-FOMC divergence days too, then the LLM is just detecting a universal bond-equity mean-reversion pattern, not a Fed-specific signal.

**SELF-ASSESSED CONFIDENCE:** Low. This is the most novel and therefore the most speculative of the three hypotheses. The mechanism (reading FOMC statements to disambiguate divergent market reactions) has not been tested in academic literature, and it requires the LLM to perform complex multi-hop causal reasoning (Fed words —> concern category —> asset class implication —> market reaction consistency check) that may be beyond current LLM reliability at temperature=0. The sample size is small (~40-60 divergence events over 15 years), making statistical significance hard to establish. Additionally, the FOMC statement language is a game of inches — the shifts are often one or two words changed from the prior statement — and the LLM may not be sensitive enough to detect these marginal shifts. The biggest risk is that the "concern classification" is simply a proxy for the hawkish/dovish dimension, which algorithmic traders already parse via keyword counts (e.g., "inflation" mentions, "patient" vs. "flexible" language). If the LLM's classification adds no information beyond a simple hawkish/dovish score, the hypothesis reduces to "fade post-FOMC equity moves on divergence days" which is a known (and likely already-arbed) pattern.

**BIGGEST WEAKNESS:** **Sample size and overfitting to a small number of historical events.** With only ~8 FOMC meetings per year and divergence events occurring at roughly 30-50% of them, the total sample is ~40-60 trades over 15 years. This is dangerously small for a machine learning approach — with 4 concern categories and 2 resolution directions (long risk/long defensives), there are effectively 8 "bins" with an expected 5-8 observations per bin. Any apparent edge could easily be a result of fitting to a handful of memorable FOMC events (e.g., the March 2020 emergency cuts, the June 2022 75bp hike, the December 2023 pivot). The backtest must use a strict walk-forward methodology where the LLM classifies each FOMC event using ONLY data available before that event (including the text of prior statements for comparison, but NOT the text of future statements). The out-of-sample period (2020-2025) must be held out completely during classification calibration. Even with these precautions, the small sample means the strategy could have a true win rate of 55% (barely positive) and still appear to have a 70% win rate by chance over 50 observations. A Bayesian approach with an informed prior (e.g., centered at 50% win rate) should be used to estimate the posterior distribution of the win rate given the observed data.
