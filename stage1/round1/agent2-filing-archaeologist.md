# Agent 2: Filing Archaeologist — Round 1 Hypotheses

---

## HYPOTHESIS 1: Risk Factor Clean Removal vs. Dirty Removal Drift

HYPOTHESIS NAME: Risk Factor Clean Removal vs. Dirty Materialization Drift
SOURCE AGENT: Filing Archaeologist
MECHANISM: When a company removes a previously disclosed risk factor from Item 1A of its 10-K or 10-Q, the market underreacts because (a) risk factor sections are dense legalese that few analysts systematically diff across filings, and (b) the market does not distinguish between "clean" removals (risk mitigated or resolved favorably — bullish) and "dirty" removals (risk materialized into an actual adverse event, so it is no longer a "risk" but a realized loss — bearish). An LLM can cross-reference the removed risk factor against (i) the Legal Proceedings section, (ii) the MD&A narrative, (iii) contemporaneous 8-K filings within +/- 30 days, and (iv) the specific language used to describe the removal (passive voice, attribution of resolution, presence/absence of quantified impact). Clean removals predict positive subsequent returns; dirty removals predict negative subsequent returns. The market treats all risk factor changes as noise because parsing their semantic nuance at scale has historically been impossible.
LLM ADVANTAGE: A rules-based NLP system can detect that a risk factor was removed, but only a frontier LLM can perform the cross-document semantic reasoning required to classify WHY it was removed. This requires: (a) extracting the removed risk factor text from the prior filing, (b) locating whether the underlying adverse event appears in the Legal Proceedings note (10-K Note X), MD&A disclosures, or contemporaneous 8-Ks, (c) classifying the removal as CLEAN (no materialized event found), DIRTY (materialized event confirmed), or AMBIGUOUS (insufficient evidence either way), and (d) reading the language surrounding the removal for deflection markers (passive voice, nominalization, agent deletion). This is a multi-hop reasoning task across 3-5 filing documents that no keyword-based system can perform. A human analyst could theoretically do it for one stock, but not for the entire Russell 3000 within the filing window.
WHY UNDERWEIGHTED: (1) Risk factor sections are widely considered boilerplate — analysts and quants alike dismiss them as CYA legal padding. (2) There is no commercial data product that classifies risk factor changes by resolution type. (3) The task requires reading 3+ filing documents per company per quarter, which is uneconomical for human teams. (4) Academic literature on risk factor disclosures focuses on COUNT of risk factors or simple LDA topic models, not cross-document resolution classification. (5) The signal is subtle — it requires distinguishing which specific risk was removed and why, which is not captured by any machine-readable XBRL tag.
HOLDING PERIOD: 1-4 weeks. The market gradually absorbs the implications of risk factor changes as analysts update models and the next earnings cycle incorporates the resolved (or materialized) risk into reported numbers. The signal should be strongest in the first 10 trading days post-filing and decay significantly after 20 trading days.
DATA REQUIREMENT: (1) SEC EDGAR Full-Index bulk downloads of all 10-K, 10-Q, and 8-K filings for Russell 3000 constituents, 2018-2025. Free via SEC.gov HTTPS (sec.gov/Archives/edgar/full-index). (2) Item 1A (Risk Factors) section extraction from 10-K and 10-Q documents — parse HTML/XML filing documents using section headers, not XBRL tags (Item 1A is not always XBRL-tagged). (3) Legal Proceedings section extraction (typically Item 3 in 10-K, or Note X in financial statement footnotes). (4) MD&A section extraction (Item 7 in 10-K, Item 2 in 10-Q). (5) 8-K item header parsing — extract ITEM numbers from the filing body (typically "Item 1.01", "Item 5.02", etc.). (6) CRSP or Yahoo Finance daily total return data for the same universe. Total data size: approximately 3-5 GB of raw filing text; all sources are free.
PROCESSING APPROACH: Step 1 — For every 10-K and 10-Q filing, use a small deterministic LLM (8B parameters, temperature=0) to extract and enumerate risk factors from Item 1A as a structured list of individually identifiable risk items. Step 2 — For each consecutive filing pair (e.g., 2023 10-K vs. 2022 10-K), embed risk factor items and compute cosine similarity to match which items correspond across filings. Items in the prior filing with no match above a similarity threshold (e.g., 0.75) are "removed." Items in the current filing with no match are "added." Step 3 — For each REMOVED risk factor, launch a multi-document reasoning pass: feed the LLM (a) the removed risk factor text, (b) the Legal Proceedings section from the current filing, (c) the MD&A from the current filing, (d) all 8-K filings from the company in the window [-30, +30] days from the filing date. The LLM classifies each removal as CLEAN, DIRTY, or AMBIGUOUS with a chain-of-thought justification. Step 4 — Construct a signal: long CLEAN-removal stocks, short DIRTY-removal stocks, equally weighted, rebalanced weekly. Step 5 — Compare against baseline factors (momentum, size, value, post-earnings drift) to ensure residual alpha is not factor-recycled.
FALSIFIABLE PREDICTION: If this edge is real: a long-short portfolio (long CLEAN removals, short DIRTY removals) should produce an annualized alpha of at least 5% after transaction costs (spread + commission) over the period 2019-2024, with an information ratio > 0.35. The effect should be monotonic: AMBIGUOUS removals should produce returns intermediate between CLEAN and DIRTY. If the edge is fake: the long-short spread should be statistically indistinguishable from zero (p > 0.10, two-sided bootstrap test) after controlling for sector, market cap, and 12-1 month momentum. Additionally, a placebo test using ADDED risk factors (which have no theoretical link to the clean/dirty mechanism) should show zero spread.
MINIMUM EFFECT SIZE: Annualized alpha > 4% after costs, information ratio > 0.3. Below this, the signal is too weak to justify the processing complexity and the 1-4 week holding period introduces too much idiosyncratic noise relative to the edge.
OUT-OF-SAMPLE PLAN: (1) Temporal holdout: train the removal-classification prompt and any threshold parameters (e.g., similarity cutoff for risk factor matching) on 2018-2021 data; test strictly on 2022-2025 data. (2) The CLEAN/DIRTY classification rubric itself should be frozen before seeing any return data — the classification definitions must be written against filing text only. (3) Cross-sectional holdout: test on Russell 2000 only (where analyst coverage is thinner and the signal should be stronger) after developing on large-cap universe. (4) Replicate on a different jurisdiction if feasible (e.g., Canadian SEDAR filings with MD&A risk factor equivalents) to test generalizability.
SELF-ASSESSED CONFIDENCE: Medium. The mechanism is theoretically sound — risk factor changes convey real information that is costly to process. The main risks are noise (many risk factor changes are cosmetic legal rewrites, not substantive) and the possibility that the market already prices the information through the underlying event (if a lawsuit is filed, the 8-K already moved the stock, making the risk factor removal in the next 10-K redundant).
BIGGEST WEAKNESS: Temporal confounding. If a risk materializes (DIRTY), the stock has likely already reacted to the materializing event (lawsuit filing, customer loss announcement, regulatory action) before the risk factor is removed from the 10-K. The risk factor removal occurs in the NEXT quarterly or annual filing, which arrives weeks or months after the event. If the market has already fully priced the event, the "DIRTY" removal signal adds nothing — it is merely an echo of already-public information. The edge thus depends on the market systematically underreacting to the initial event, with the risk factor removal in the filing serving as a confirming signal that triggers further price discovery. If market reactions to 8-K events are efficiently complete, this hypothesis breaks.

## HYPOTHESIS 2: Critical Audit Matter (CAM) Topic Cluster Evolution as Distress Early Warning

HYPOTHESIS NAME: CAM Expansion Velocity as Distress Precursor
SOURCE AGENT: Filing Archaeologist
MECHANISM: Critical Audit Matters (CAMs) — required in auditor reports since 2019 (PCAOB AS 3101) — are the issues that the external auditor identified as involving especially challenging, subjective, or complex judgment. When a company's auditor adds a NEW CAM that was not present in the prior year's report, particularly one that falls into a novel topic cluster not previously associated with that company, it signals that the auditor has identified a deteriorating area of the business that requires heightened scrutiny. Conversely, when a CAM is dropped, it signals that the underlying issue has been resolved or is no longer material to audit risk. The market underweights CAM changes because: (a) CAMs are buried in the auditor's report (a section most investors skip), (b) CAM additions are subtle — auditors use standardized language that obscures the severity gradient, and (c) there is no established quantitative framework for measuring CAM "velocity" (rate of topic expansion over time). An LLM can extract CAM paragraphs, embed them into a semantic space, cluster CAM topics across the entire market, and then measure at the company level whether the auditor is expanding into new topic territories — a signal of escalating accounting complexity that precedes visible financial deterioration by 1-3 quarters.
LLM ADVANTAGE: This requires (a) extracting CAM descriptions from auditor reports with high precision despite formatting variability across audit firms (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG each format their reports differently), (b) embedding CAM paragraphs and clustering them into a market-wide taxonomy of CAM topics (e.g., revenue recognition, goodwill impairment, contingent liabilities, inventory valuation, acquisition accounting, going concern), (c) detecting when a company's CAM set expands into a new cluster that is rare for its sector — this is a cross-sectional anomaly detection problem in embedding space, and (d) measuring the "topic velocity" — how many new CAM clusters has the auditor added in the last 2 years relative to the company's own history and relative to sector peers. A rules-based system fails because CAM language is not standardized across audit firms — the same underlying issue (e.g., revenue cut-off) is described differently by each firm. Only semantic embedding + clustering can identify that Deloitte's "revenue recognition — timing of satisfaction of performance obligations" and PwC's "accuracy of revenue recorded near period end" refer to the same audit concern.
WHY UNDERWEIGHTED: (1) CAM reporting is new (mandatory for large accelerated filers since mid-2019, for all public companies since Dec 2020), so the academic and practitioner literature is thin — most studies focus on whether CAMs affect audit fees or auditor liability, not stock returns. (2) CAM data is not available in any standard financial database (Bloomberg, FactSet, Compustat do not systematically parse CAMs). (3) The signal requires comparing a company's current CAMs to its historical CAMs AND to sector peers' CAMs simultaneously — a multi-dimensional comparison that is computationally non-trivial without embeddings. (4) Investors and analysts overwhelmingly ignore the auditor's report — it is viewed as a binary pass/fail signal (unqualified vs. going concern), not a rich source of continuous signal.
HOLDING PERIOD: 1-6 months. Auditor-identified problems typically manifest in reported financials with a 1-3 quarter lag. The auditor sees the problem during the audit (completed ~45-90 days after fiscal year end), the CAM appears in the 10-K, and the market fully appreciates the implications only when the deterioration shows up in subsequent earnings. The optimal entry window is the 20 trading days after 10-K filing; exit when the next quarterly earnings are reported or at 6 months, whichever comes first.
DATA REQUIREMENT: (1) All 10-K filings for US-listed companies from 2019-2025 (CAMs were phased in starting 2019 for large accelerated filers). Source: SEC EDGAR, free. Must download full filing text (HTML/XML), not just the XBRL instance. (2) Auditor's report section extraction — the CAM paragraphs appear within the audit opinion, typically under headings like "Critical Audit Matters" or "Critical Audit Matters" with subheadings for each CAM. (3) CRSP/Compustat via WRDS (free for academic access) or Yahoo Finance daily prices (free, retail-accessible). (4) Sector classification: GICS sectors from Wikipedia/Yahoo Finance (free). (5) Filing date metadata from EDGAR index files — must use the ACCEPTANCE DATE (when the SEC accepted the filing) as the point-in-time date, not the period-end date.
PROCESSING APPROACH: Step 1 — Extract CAM paragraphs from each 10-K filing using section-header parsing. Each CAM is a named subsection within the auditor's report. Store CAM text, company CIK, filing date, and fiscal period end date. Step 2 — Using a small deterministic LLM (8B params, temperature=0), embed each CAM paragraph into a 1024-dimensional vector (or use a dedicated text embedding model). Cluster CAMs market-wide using HDBSCAN to build a taxonomy of 20-40 CAM topic clusters, labeled by the LLM reading representative CAMs from each cluster. Step 3 — For each company-filing, construct a binary CAM cluster presence vector. Compute (a) NEW_CLUSTERS: clusters present now that were not present in the prior year's filing, and (b) DROPPED_CLUSTERS: clusters present in the prior year but absent now. Step 4 — Construct a signal: CAM_EXPANSION = (number of new clusters) / (total clusters in current filing). Rank companies within each sector. The top quintile of CAM_EXPANSION is the short basket, the bottom quintile (CAM contraction or stability) is the long basket. Step 5 — Control for concurrent financial deterioration by including reported revenue change, earnings surprise, and accruals as covariates. The hypothesis is that CAM expansion predicts returns ABOVE AND BEYOND what concurrent financial metrics already reveal.
FALSIFIABLE PREDICTION: If this edge is real: the top quintile of CAM_EXPANSION (most new CAM clusters) should underperform the bottom quintile by at least 6% annualized over a 6-month holding period post-filing, after controlling for size, book-to-market, momentum, and accruals. The effect should be strongest for companies where the new CAM is in a cluster that is RARE for their sector (e.g., inventory valuation CAM for a software company). If the edge is fake: the return spread should be statistically insignificant (p > 0.10) after controlling for the concurrent deterioration in reported financials (i.e., CAM expansion adds no predictive power beyond what you can see in the income statement and balance sheet). A placebo test: shuffle CAM assignments across companies within the same sector and filing year; the shuffled signal should produce zero spread.
MINIMUM EFFECT SIZE: Annualized alpha > 5% after costs, with an information ratio > 0.3 over the 6-month holding window. The holding period is long enough that transaction costs are modest (annual turnover of ~2-3x), so a 5% net alpha threshold is reasonable.
OUT-OF-SAMPLE PLAN: (1) Temporal split: develop CAM taxonomy and embedding approach on 2019-2021 filings; validate on 2022-2025 filings. Critically, the clustering labels and sector-rarity definitions must be frozen from the training period. (2) The LLM prompt for CAM extraction must be tested on a held-out set of 100 auditor reports from different audit firms and manually validated for extraction accuracy before deployment. (3) Cross-regime: test performance separately in rising-rate (2022-2023) vs. falling-rate (2019-2020, late 2024+) regimes — CAM signals may be stronger in deteriorating credit conditions. (4) Cross-auditor: test whether the signal varies by audit firm (Big 4 vs. non-Big 4) — smaller audit firms may have more variance in CAM reporting, potentially adding noise.
SELF-ASSESSED CONFIDENCE: Medium-Low. The CAM dataset is young (only ~6 years of data), which limits sample size for statistical power. The mechanism is plausible but depends on auditors being faster at identifying problems than the market is at pricing them — which may not hold for large, heavily analyzed stocks. The signal is more likely to work in the small/mid-cap universe where analyst coverage is thinner and auditor reports receive even less attention.
BIGGEST WEAKNESS: Sample size and statistical power. CAMs have existed for large accelerated filers since mid-2019 and for all public companies only since December 2020. For a 6-month holding period, we have at most ~10 non-overlapping holding periods. Company-level CAM changes are infrequent — most companies have stable CAM sets year-over-year. The number of companies with genuine CAM expansion events (addition of a new CAM cluster) may be too small (estimated 5-15% of filings per year) to construct a diversified portfolio. With an estimated 300-500 CAM-expansion events per year across the entire US market, a long-short quintile portfolio may hold only 60-100 positions on each side, leading to high idiosyncratic variance. This could make the signal statistically undetectable even if economically real. The hypothesis may be untestable until another 5+ years of CAM data accumulate.

## HYPOTHESIS 3: 8-K Item 5.02 Officer Departure Language Escalation Preceding Adverse Events

HYPOTHESIS NAME: 8-K Departure Language Severity as Stealth Warning
SOURCE AGENT: Filing Archaeologist
MECHANISM: When a company files an 8-K Item 5.02 (Departure of Directors or Certain Officers; Election of Directors; Appointment of Certain Officers), the market's reaction is primarily driven by WHO left (CEO, CFO, key executive) and the binary fact of departure. However, the SPECIFIC LANGUAGE used to describe the departure contains a rich gradient of severity that the market ignores. Departures described with "mutual agreement," "personal reasons," or "to pursue other opportunities" are fundamentally different from departures described with "resigned effective immediately," "departure follows an internal investigation," or departures accompanied by concurrent Item 5.02 disclosures about "appointment of certain officers" under emergency succession provisions. Furthermore, departures where the company does NOT issue a thank-you statement, or where the thank-you statement is perfunctory (fewer than 30 words) versus effusive (80+ words with specific accomplishments cited), signal internal conflict versus amicable transition. An LLM can extract these language features from Item 5.02 filings at scale and construct a "Departure Severity Score" that predicts elevated probability of adverse subsequent events (earnings restatement, material weakness disclosure, SEC investigation, class action lawsuit filing, or significant negative earnings surprise) over the subsequent 3-12 months.
LLM ADVANTAGE: This is a fine-grained natural language understanding task requiring: (a) extraction of the departure description paragraph from the unstructured Item 5.02 disclosure (which varies widely in format across companies and law firms), (b) classification of the departure narrative along multiple latent dimensions — suddenness ("effective immediately" vs. "effective [future date 30+ days out]"), warmth of farewell language (presence/absence, length, specificity of the thank-you), attribution of cause (stated vs. unstated, internal vs. external), co-occurrence with investigation language, and whether temporary/interim replacements are named in the same filing, (c) construction of a composite severity score from these features, and (d) calibration of that score against a database of subsequent adverse events. No rules-based system can capture "warmth of farewell" or distinguish a perfunctory "we thank Mr. X for his service" from a detailed "Mr. X led the company through its IPO, oversaw revenue growth from $100M to $500M, and built a world-class management team." The market prices the WHO but not the HOW — and the HOW is where the LLM edge lies.
WHY UNDERWEIGHTED: (1) Item 5.02 filings are frequent (thousands per year across the US market), and most are innocuous retirements or planned transitions — the base rate of "problematic" departures is low enough that the market has learned to mostly ignore 5.02 filings unless a CEO or CFO is involved. (2) The language signals are inherently fuzzy and require judgment — there is no Bloomberg field for "thank-you paragraph word count" or "presence of 'effective immediately' language." (3) Institutional investors and analysts already process WHO and sometimes WHY (if the press release covers it), but the systematic linguistic gradient within the SEC filing itself is not captured by any commercial dataset. (4) The signal is probabilistic, not deterministic — most "effective immediately" departures do NOT precede a disaster, so a naive rule ("short all effective-immediately departures") would have a terrible hit rate. The LLM's advantage is weighting multiple weak linguistic signals into a composite that is more predictive than any single feature alone.
HOLDING PERIOD: 1-6 months. The adverse events that departure language severity anticipates (restatements, material weaknesses, investigations, lawsuits, earnings disappointments) typically unfold over subsequent quarters. The position should be sized as a tail-risk hedge rather than a concentrated short — these are low-probability, high-severity events. The optimal approach may be buying out-of-the-money puts on high-severity departure stocks rather than shorting common equity, as the payoff profile is asymmetric (most will do nothing, a few will collapse).
DATA REQUIREMENT: (1) All 8-K Item 5.02 filings for US-listed companies, 2015-2025. Source: SEC EDGAR Full-Index, filtering by 8-K form type and then parsing the item headers within each filing to isolate Item 5.02 disclosures. Free. (2) The departure description text — the paragraph(s) following the item heading that describe the circumstances of departure. Must handle multi-officer disclosures (multiple departures in a single 8-K). (3) Subsequent adverse event data: (a) 8-K Item 4.02 filings (non-reliance on previously issued financials / restatements), (b) 8-K Item 1.01 and Item 5.02 emergency appointments, (c) SEC litigation releases (sec.gov/litigation) for class action filings, (d) earnings surprise data from Yahoo Finance or EDGAR 10-Q/10-K filings (comparing reported EPS to prior guidance). (4) CRSP or Yahoo Finance daily prices. (5) Officer identity data — need to identify WHO departed (CEO, CFO, CAO, CLO, COO, Board member) to control for the known "who" effect. Source: the 8-K filing text itself (the LLM extracts the officer title).
PROCESSING APPROACH: Step 1 — Extract every Item 5.02 departure event from 8-K filings using a deterministic LLM (8B, temperature=0). For each departure, extract: company CIK, filing date, officer name, officer title, departure description paragraph(s), effective date of departure, reason stated (if any), and the full thank-you/farewell paragraph (if present). Step 2 — Using the LLM, code each departure along 8+ binary or ordinal features: (a) SUDDEN: "effective immediately" or effective date <= 7 days from filing? (b) NO_REASON: is no reason stated for departure? (c) THANK_SHORT: thank-you paragraph < 30 words or absent? (d) INVESTIGATION: does the filing or contemporaneous 8-K mention an internal investigation, audit committee review, or regulatory inquiry? (e) CONCURRENT_APPT: is a replacement named in the same filing (sign of pre-planned succession) vs. "the board has begun a search" (sign of unplanned departure)? (f) MULTI_DEPARTURE: are multiple officers departing simultaneously? (g) CFO_INVOLVED: is the CFO or CAO among the departing officers (accounting/finance roles are more concerning)? (h) RETIREMENT_AGE: is the officer over 62 and described as "retiring"? Step 3 — Construct a Departure Severity Score via a weighted sum (or train a simple logistic regression on a 2015-2019 training set with the target being "adverse event within 12 months"). Freeze weights. Step 4 — On the 2020-2025 test set, rank all departure events by severity score. The short basket is the top decile of severity; the long basket is the bottom decile (benign departures). Step 5 — Measure return spreads and, separately, measure the hit rate for specific adverse event types (restatements, material weaknesses, SEC investigations, class actions).
FALSIFIABLE PREDICTION: If this edge is real: the top decile of Departure Severity Score should underperform the bottom decile by at least 8% annualized (or, more precisely, should have a statistically significant negative mean excess return of -5% or worse over the 6 months post-filing). Additionally, the top decile should have at least a 3x higher incidence rate of subsequent adverse events (restatements, material weaknesses, SEC investigations, or -20%+ earnings surprises) compared to the bottom decile over a 12-month horizon. The relationship should be monotonic across deciles. If the edge is fake: (a) no monotonic relationship between severity decile and subsequent returns, (b) the incidence rate of adverse events in the top decile is not statistically distinguishable from the overall base rate (p > 0.10, chi-squared test), and (c) a score constructed from purely random weights on the same features should produce identical (null) results — i.e., the specific weighting of features matters.
MINIMUM EFFECT SIZE: Annualized alpha > 6% after costs for the long-short spread, OR a top-decile adverse event incidence rate that is at least 2.5x the base rate (economically meaningful as a screening tool to avoid toxic stocks, even if not directly tradeable as a long-short portfolio due to the asymmetric payoff structure). The primary economic value may be as a negative screen ("don't own stocks with high-severity departures") rather than as an active short signal.
OUT-OF-SAMPLE PLAN: (1) Strict temporal split: train the severity score weights on 2015-2019 departure events and their subsequent outcomes; test on 2020-2025. No parameter updating after seeing test data. (2) The LLM feature extraction prompts must be written and frozen on 2015-2019 data only — no prompt refinement after seeing 2020+ outcomes. (3) Cross-sectional robustness: test separately on large-cap (>$10B), mid-cap ($2-10B), and small-cap (<$2B) universes. The signal should be stronger in small caps where the departure is a larger proportion of the information environment. (4) Out-of-sample by event type: if the score was calibrated against "any adverse event within 12 months," test whether it specifically predicts each adverse event type individually — the score should NOT cherry-pick; if it only predicts class action lawsuits but not restatements, that suggests overfitting to a particular event type. (5) Replicate on Canadian SEDAR and UK RNS departure announcements to test whether the linguistic features are jurisdiction-invariant.
SELF-ASSESSED CONFIDENCE: Medium. The mechanism is grounded in well-established organizational behavior research (abrupt executive departures without clear explanation predict internal turmoil) and forensic accounting literature (linguistic markers in corporate disclosures predict fraud and restatements). The main challenge is base rate: problematic departures are rare, so even a strong signal may generate a high false-positive rate that makes the strategy economically unviable as a standalone short portfolio. The economic value is more likely as a screening tool (avoid these stocks in a long portfolio) than as a directional bet.
BIGGEST WEAKNESS: Base rate and false positive problem. Even in the top decile of severity, the absolute probability of a subsequent adverse event is likely low (estimated 10-20% over 12 months vs. a 3-5% base rate). This means 80-90% of "high severity" departure stocks do NOT experience a visible adverse event in the following year. As a short-selling strategy, this is death by negative carry — you pay borrow costs and suffer upward drift on 80-90% of positions while waiting for the 10-20% that crash. The strategy cannot work as a naive short basket; it requires either (a) option-based implementation with defined risk (buying puts, which has its own theta decay problem), (b) combination with a catalyst-timing signal to narrow the window, or (c) use exclusively as a negative screen for long portfolios (foregone upside, but avoided blowups). Additionally, there is a selection bias problem: companies with truly egregious problems (fraud, imminent bankruptcy) may not file clean 8-Ks at all — the CEO may simply be terminated without a properly filed Item 5.02, or the filing may be delayed beyond the 4-business-day requirement, meaning the worst cases are missing from the dataset. The signal we can construct may only capture the "gray zone" cases, not the black swans.
